AIGP Exam Prep · Week 1

Day 3 — Lifecycle Policies

Domain I · Competency I.C · Establish policies and procedures to apply throughout the AI life cycle
29 May 2026 6–8 exam questions ~15 min read Domain I of IV
BOK Performance Indicators — I.C

These are the exact competencies the AIGP exam tests for this session. Everything else on this card builds toward demonstrating these.

  • I.C — PI 1 Create and implement policies to ensure oversight and accountability across all AI life cycle stages — including use case assessment, risk management, ethics by design, data acquisition and use, model and system development, training and testing, deployment and monitoring, documentation and reporting, and incident management.
  • I.C — PI 2 Evaluate and update existing policies (e.g., data privacy, security, data governance, and intellectual property) for AI.
  • I.C — PI 3 Create, update and implement policies, assessments and contracts to manage third-party risk (e.g., procurement, supply chain, human resources and acceptable use).
Key Concepts ▾
1. The AI Life Cycle and Why Policy Must Cover All Stages ▾

The BOK identifies a set of stages that together constitute the AI life cycle: use case assessment, risk management, data acquisition and use, model and system development, training and testing, deployment and monitoring, documentation and reporting, and incident management. Governance gaps arise when policy coverage stops at deployment — leaving monitoring, incident response, and end-of-life without oversight.

The term "ethics by design" in PI 1 signals that ethical considerations must be addressed at the use case assessment stage — before a line of code is written. If risk and ethics only enter the picture at testing or deployment, the design decisions that created the risk have already been locked in.

Why it matters for governance: A policy that covers only deployment and monitoring is not a lifecycle policy. The exam tests whether you can identify where a governance gap exists in the lifecycle and which PI is implicated.
KEY TERMS Ethics by design: Embedding ethical review and risk consideration into the earliest stages of AI development (use case assessment), not as a retrofit after build.

Incident management: Policies and procedures for identifying, responding to, and remediating failures or harms caused by AI systems during or after deployment.
2. Evaluating and Updating Existing Policies for AI ▾

Most organisations have existing policies — data privacy, cybersecurity, intellectual property, data governance — written before AI was deployed at scale. PI 2 requires governance professionals to evaluate these policies and update them for AI. This is not optional, and it is not solved by writing a separate "AI policy" that sits alongside the old one.

For example: a data privacy policy written in 2019 may address consent for data collection but say nothing about using personal data to train a model, automated profiling, or inference attacks. That policy must be updated. Similarly, an intellectual property policy must address AI-generated content ownership and the legality of using third-party datasets for training.

Why it matters for governance: Exam distractors will suggest creating a parallel AI policy is sufficient. The correct AIGP answer is that existing policies must themselves be evaluated and updated — integration, not addition.
KEY TERMS Policy evaluation: Systematic review of existing organisational policies to identify gaps, conflicts, or omissions created by the introduction of AI systems.

Inference attack: A technique where an adversary extracts information about training data from a deployed AI model — a privacy risk not addressed by traditional data collection policies.
3. Third-Party Risk Management for AI ▾

Modern AI systems rarely exist in isolation. They depend on externally sourced components: foundation models, training datasets, cloud APIs, human-in-the-loop labelling services, and software libraries. PI 3 identifies four vectors through which third-party risk must be managed:

  • Procurement: Due diligence before acquiring an AI system or model from a vendor.
  • Supply chain: Understanding the provenance of every component in your AI stack.
  • Human resources: Policies governing how employees interact with, use, and are affected by AI.
  • Acceptable use: Rules governing which third-party AI tools employees may use and what data they may submit.

PI 3 specifies that this management happens through policies, assessments, and contracts — all three, not just one.

Why it matters for governance: The exam will present scenarios where a vendor changes practices without notice or where an employee submits confidential data to a public AI tool. Knowing which of the four vectors applies — and which policy instrument to use — is the tested skill.
KEY TERMS Supply chain risk: Risk arising from the components, data, or services that make up the AI system but are not directly controlled by the deploying organisation.

Acceptable use policy (AUP): A policy defining permitted and prohibited uses of AI tools by employees.
4. Documentation and Reporting as a Lifecycle Stage ▾

The BOK treats documentation and reporting as a distinct lifecycle stage — not an administrative afterthought. Documentation enables: retrospective review of decisions, audit trails for regulators, evidence of compliance, and forensic investigation when incidents occur.

Why it matters for governance: Exam questions on documentation governance typically test whether candidates understand it is a policy requirement under I.C, not a technical or quality-assurance matter only.

Worked Scenario ▾
Situation

A global insurance company uses an AI model to triage and prioritise claims. The model was licensed from a third-party insurtech vendor 18 months ago. The vendor recently updated the underlying model without notifying the insurer. The audit identified three issues: (1) the vendor contract does not require notification of model updates; (2) the data governance policy does not address use of policyholder data to retrain the model; (3) there is no documented escalation path when the model flags a claim as high-risk.

Analysis

Issue 1: Third-party contract gap → I.C PI 3. Fix: update contract to require notification of material model changes. I.C PI 3

Issue 2: Existing data governance policy not updated for AI → I.C PI 2. Fix: update the policy, not add a parallel one. I.C PI 2

Issue 3: No incident management policy linking monitoring to escalation → I.C PI 1 lifecycle coverage gap. I.C PI 1
