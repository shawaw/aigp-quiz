Audits & Transparency

Domain III · Competency III.C  |  Exam weight: 8–10 questions
BOK III.C — Performance Indicators (verbatim)
  1. III.C.1 Assess readiness, and prepare for release into production (e.g., creating the model card and satisfying conformity requirements).
  2. III.C.2 Conduct continuous monitoring of the AI system, and establish a regular schedule for maintenance, updates and retraining.
  3. III.C.3 Conduct periodic activities to assess the AI system's performance, reliability and safety (e.g., audits, red teaming, threat modeling and security testing).
  4. III.C.4 Manage and document incidents, issues and risks.
  5. III.C.5 Collaborate with cross-functional stakeholders to understand why incidents arise from AI systems (e.g., brittleness, lack of robustness, lack of quality data, insufficient testing, and model or data drift).
  6. III.C.6 Make public disclosures to meet transparency obligations (e.g., technical documentation, instructions for use to deployers and post-market monitoring plans).
Concepts Key Concepts ▾
III.C.3 — Periodic Assessment Activities

Continuous monitoring (III.C.2) tracks real-time operational performance. Periodic assessment (III.C.3) is a separate, structured obligation — it proactively tests and evaluates the system at defined intervals, not just when alerts fire. The two obligations coexist; neither replaces the other.

Why it matters on the exam: Candidates frequently conflate monitoring and periodic assessment. The exam tests whether you can assign the right activity to the right PI and distinguish the purpose of each.

Activities under III.C.3
Audits — Structured, independent reviews of system performance, compliance, and governance documentation against defined criteria. Scheduled, not continuous.
Red teaming — Adversarial simulation where a team acts as attackers to find exploitable vulnerabilities, misuse pathways, or failure modes that internal testing missed.
Threat modeling — Systematic identification and evaluation of potential attack vectors and failure modes. Typically revisited at periodic intervals or when the system changes significantly.
Security testing — Formal testing of resistance to adversarial inputs, injection attacks, and infrastructure vulnerabilities.
III.C.6 — Transparency Obligations & Public Disclosures

III.C.6 governs what the AI developer or provider must make externally available. The BOK enumerates three specific categories. Absent any one of them, the obligation is unmet. This is distinct from internal documentation obligations (III.A.5, III.B.5) and from regulatory breach notification under privacy law (II.A.3).

Why it matters on the exam: Questions test whether you know the exact scope of III.C.6 and can identify which element is missing in a scenario — particularly instructions for use to deployers, which is the most commonly absent element when deployers misapply a model.

Required public disclosures under III.C.6
Technical documentation — Architecture, training approach, evaluation methodology, and known limitations. Must be disclosed, not treated as purely proprietary.
Instructions for use to deployers — Operational constraints, intended use scope, and risk mitigations that deployers need to integrate the system correctly. The most frequently missing element in exam scenarios involving misapplication.
Post-market monitoring plans — The provider's plan for tracking system performance, safety signals, and emerging risks after release. Required before issues arise, not only in response to them.
III.C.4 & III.C.5 — Incident Management and Root Cause Analysis

III.C.4 requires documenting incidents regardless of resolution status — there is no carve-out for minor or self-resolving incidents. III.C.5 requires cross-functional collaboration to understand root causes, not just technical investigation in isolation.

Why it matters on the exam: A common exam trap presents a resolved incident and asks whether documentation is still required (it is, under III.C.4). Separately, scenarios present unexplained performance failures and ask you to identify the correct root cause category from the BOK list.

Root causes enumerated in III.C.5
Brittleness — Model fails outside a narrow training distribution; collapses on edge cases not seen in training.
Lack of robustness — Model is susceptible to adversarial inputs or distribution shifts that exploit architectural weaknesses.
Lack of quality data — Training or inference data is incomplete, biased, or corrupt, producing systematic errors.
Insufficient testing — Pre-deployment testing failed to cover production scenarios — a design-phase failure surfacing operationally.
Model or data drift — The statistical relationship between inputs and outputs has shifted since training; the real world has changed while the model has not.

Scenario Worked Scenario — Pharma Diagnostic AI ▾
Situation
A pharmaceutical company releases an AI diagnostic support tool to hospital deployers. Technical documentation is published, but no instructions for use and no post-market monitoring plan are issued. Six months post-deployment, hospitals report unexpected diagnostic errors in a specific patient subpopulation. Investigation reveals the training data no longer reflects the demographic mix of current patient intake.
Role
You are the AI Governance Lead responsible for III.C compliance across the product lifecycle.
Analysis
At release, the company had two unmet obligations under III.C.6: instructions for use to deployers and a post-market monitoring plan were not published. Hospitals therefore had no documented operational constraints and no monitoring protocol from the provider. The errors constitute an incident under III.C.4 — requiring documentation regardless of whether the issue is subsequently resolved. The root cause under III.C.5 is model or data drift. A periodic audit or red teaming exercise under III.C.3 conducted before the demographic shift became acute might have surfaced the vulnerability earlier.
Outcome
Corrective action must: (1) publish instructions for use and post-market monitoring plan retroactively per III.C.6; (2) document the incident, its impact, and residual risks per III.C.4; (3) facilitate cross-functional root cause investigation per III.C.5; (4) schedule a red team exercise and formal audit per III.C.3 for the next product cycle. The missing instructions for use was the single most preventable failure.
Recall Quick Recall (click to reveal) ▾
What three items must be publicly disclosed under III.C.6?↓
Technical documentation, instructions for use to deployers, and post-market monitoring plans.
How does red teaming differ from security testing under III.C.3?↓
Red teaming simulates adversarial misuse by acting as an attacker. Security testing uses formal methodology to probe infrastructure and input vulnerabilities. Both are periodic assessment activities under III.C.3.
Does III.C.4 require incident documentation if the incident is fully resolved?↓
Yes. III.C.4 requires managing and documenting incidents, issues and risks. There is no exemption for resolved incidents.
Name the five root causes of AI incidents listed in III.C.5.↓
Brittleness, lack of robustness, lack of quality data, insufficient testing, and model or data drift.
What is the key distinction between III.C.2 and III.C.3?↓
III.C.2 is continuous monitoring (real-time, alert-driven). III.C.3 is periodic structured assessment (scheduled, proactive) — audits, red teaming, threat modeling, security testing.
When a deployer misapplies an AI system beyond its intended scope, which III.C.6 element was most likely missing?↓
Instructions for use to deployers — these specify operational constraints, intended scope, and risk mitigations that prevent misapplication.
What does a model card document, per III.C.1?↓
Intended use, performance characteristics, and limitations — enabling informed deployment decisions.
What triggers a retraining schedule review under III.C.2?↓
Changed operating conditions (macroeconomic shifts, demographic changes, product updates) — not only when alert thresholds are breached. The schedule itself must remain fit for purpose.
