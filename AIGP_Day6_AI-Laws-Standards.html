CONCEPTS Key Concepts ▸
EU AI Act — Risk Classification Framework (II.C.1)▸

The EU AI Act stratifies AI systems into four risk tiers. The tier determines which obligations apply — not the technology itself, but the use case and context of deployment.

The four tiers
Prohibited — Banned outright. Examples: social scoring by public authorities, real-time remote biometric ID in public spaces (with narrow exceptions), subliminal manipulation, exploitation of vulnerable groups.
High-Risk — Permitted but heavily regulated. Includes AI in: critical infrastructure, education/vocational training, employment/HR, essential services (credit, insurance), law enforcement, migration/asylum, administration of justice.
Limited-Risk — Transparency obligations only. Chatbots must disclose AI nature. Deepfakes must be labelled. Emotion recognition systems must notify users.
Minimal-Risk — No specific obligations. AI spam filters, AI in video games.
High-Risk AI — Core Requirements (II.C.2 + II.C.3)▸
Risk Management System — Continuous, iterative process throughout the lifecycle.
Data Governance — Training, validation and testing data must meet quality criteria; bias examined; data gaps identified.
Technical Documentation — Must be drawn up before market placement and kept up to date.
Record-Keeping / Logging — Automatic logs to enable traceability, monitoring and post-market surveillance.
Transparency & Instructions for Use — Deployers must receive clear instructions. End-users must be informed they are interacting with AI where relevant.
Human Oversight — Measures allowing humans to monitor, intervene, override or halt the system. Must be built in by the provider; operationalised by the deployer.
Quality Management System — Providers must implement a QMS covering design, development, testing and post-market monitoring.
Exam trap: "Conformity assessment" for most high-risk AI is self-assessment (internal control). Third-party assessment is required only for biometric identification and certain safety-critical categories.
Role-Based Obligations — Provider vs Deployer (II.C.6)▸
Provider — Develops and places the AI system on the market. Bears the heaviest obligations: technical documentation, conformity assessment, CE marking, post-market monitoring, registration in EU database.
Deployer — Uses the AI system in a professional context. Must: use systems per provider instructions, implement human oversight measures, conduct a fundamental rights impact assessment (FRIA) where required, keep logs, inform affected persons.
Importer — Places a third-country AI system on the EU market. Must verify conformity assessment was done; provider's obligations are met; technical documentation available.
Distributor — Makes the system available without modifying it. Must verify CE marking and declaration of conformity exist before distribution.
A deployer who modifies a high-risk AI system becomes a provider for that modified system — triggering full provider obligations. This is a common exam scenario.
General-Purpose AI (GPAI) Models (II.C.4)▸
All GPAI providers must — Provide technical documentation and instructions for downstream providers; comply with EU copyright law; publish a summary of training data.
GPAI with systemic risk — Models trained with >10²⁵ FLOPs face additional obligations: adversarial testing (red-teaming), serious incident reporting to the EU AI Office, cybersecurity measures, energy efficiency reporting.
Open-source GPAI — Generally lighter obligations; systemic risk rules apply regardless of open-source status if the FLOP threshold is met.
Enforcement Framework & Penalties (II.C.5)▸
Prohibited AI violations — Up to €35 million or 7% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher.
Other obligations violations — Up to €15 million or 3% of global annual turnover.
Incorrect information to authorities — Up to €7.5 million or 1.5% of global annual turnover.
Oversight architecture — EU AI Office (Commission level) for GPAI. National competent authorities (NCAs) for AI systems. Market surveillance authorities enforce at member-state level.
NIST AI Risk Management Framework (II.D.2)▸
GOVERN — Foundational. Establishes culture, policies, roles and accountability for risk management. Applies across the other three functions.
MAP — Identify and classify AI risks. Context setting: use case, stakeholders, affected individuals, risk categories.
MEASURE — Analyse and assess identified risks. Metrics, testing, evaluation methods, benchmarks.
MANAGE — Prioritise and treat risks. Implement controls, monitor outcomes, respond to incidents.
GOVERN is not a fifth step — it runs in parallel with MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE. This distinction appears in exam questions.
OECD AI Principles (II.D.1)▸
5 Principles for trustworthy AI — (1) Inclusive growth, sustainable development and well-being; (2) Human-centred values and fairness; (3) Transparency and explainability; (4) Robustness, security and safety; (5) Accountability.
5 Recommendations for policy — Directed at governments: invest in R&D, develop trusted data infrastructure, foster interoperable governance, build human capacity, enable international cooperation.
OECD AI Policy Observatory — Tracks national AI policies and strategies globally. Not a regulatory body — an information and monitoring function.
ISO AI Standards (II.D.3)▸
ISO/IEC 22989 — AI concepts and terminology. The definitional standard — provides common language for all other AI standards. No certification against this standard.
ISO/IEC 42001 — AI Management System (AIMS) standard. Certifiable. Defines requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining and continually improving an AI management system.
ISO/IEC 42005 — AI system impact assessment. Guidance on conducting impact assessments for AI systems.
Exam shortcut: 22989 = language, 42001 = management system (certifiable), 42005 = impact assessment. If a question asks which standard an organisation would certify against, the answer is 42001.

RECALL Quick Recall — Click to Reveal ▸
What penalty tier applies to violations of prohibited AI rules under the EU AI Act? ▾
€35 million or 7% of global annual turnover — whichever is higher.
A deployer substantially modifies a high-risk AI system. What is their new status? ▾
They become a provider for the modified system and must fulfil all provider obligations, including technical documentation, conformity assessment and registration.
What are the four NIST AI RMF core functions? ▾
GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE. GOVERN is foundational and runs in parallel with the other three — it is not a sequential step.
Which ISO standard would an organisation certify against for their AI management system? ▾
ISO/IEC 42001 — the AI Management System standard. ISO 22989 and 42005 are not certifiable.
At what FLOP threshold does a GPAI model acquire systemic-risk obligations under the EU AI Act? ▾
10²⁵ FLOPs used in training. Additional obligations include: adversarial testing, serious incident reporting to the EU AI Office, cybersecurity measures and energy efficiency reporting.
Name the five OECD Principles on AI. ▾
(1) Inclusive growth and well-being; (2) Human-centred values and fairness; (3) Transparency and explainability; (4) Robustness, security and safety; (5) Accountability.
What transparency obligation applies to a limited-risk AI chatbot? ▾
It must disclose to users that they are interacting with an AI system — unless this is obvious from context.
What does ISO 42005 cover? ▾
AI system impact assessment — guidance on identifying who is affected by an AI system, what harms may arise, and how to mitigate them.
