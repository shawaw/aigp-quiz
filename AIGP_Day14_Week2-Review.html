Domain III Review — Governing AI Development
Competencies III.A · III.B · III.C  |  Exam weight: 20–26 questions  |  ~40 min read
BOK Performance Indicators — Domains III.A · III.B · III.C
III.A.1 Define the business context and use case of the AI system.
III.A.2 Perform or review an impact assessment on the AI system.
III.A.3 Apply the policies, procedures, best practices and ethical considerations to designing and building the AI system (e.g., purpose of AI, requirements gathering, architecture and model selection, human oversight, data analysis, metric and threshold evaluation, stakeholder engagement and feedback, and operational controls).
III.A.4 Identify and manage the internal and external risks and contributing factors related to designing and building the AI model and system (e.g., using probability/severity harms matrix, using a risk mitigation hierarchy, stakeholder mapping, use-case evaluation, benchmarking, and pre-deployment pilots and testing).
III.A.5 Document the designing and building process (e.g., to establish compliance and manage risks).
III.B.1 Establish and follow the requirements for data governance (e.g., assess and document lawful rights to collect and use data, and assess data quality, quantity, integrity and fit-for-purpose).
III.B.2 Establish and document data lineage and provenance.
III.B.3 Plan and perform training and testing of the AI model and system (e.g., unit, integration, validation, performance, security, bias and interpretability).
III.B.4 Identify and manage issues and risks during training and testing of the AI model and system.
III.B.5 Document the training and testing process (e.g., to validate results, establish compliance and manage risks).
III.C.1 Assess readiness, and prepare for release into production (e.g., creating the model card and satisfying conformity requirements).
III.C.2 Conduct continuous monitoring of the AI system, and establish a regular schedule for maintenance, updates and retraining.
III.C.3 Conduct periodic activities to assess the AI system's performance, reliability and safety (e.g., audits, red teaming, threat modeling and security testing).
III.C.4 Manage and document incidents, issues and risks.
III.C.5 Collaborate with cross-functional stakeholders to understand why incidents arise from AI systems (e.g., brittleness, lack of robustness, lack of quality data, insufficient testing, and model or data drift).
III.C.6 Make public disclosures to meet transparency obligations (e.g., technical documentation, instructions for use to deployers and post-market monitoring plans).
Concepts Domain III — Three Governance Stages ▾
III.A — Govern the Design & Build Stage

III.A governs the earliest phase of AI development — before a single training run occurs. Its five PIs form a logical sequence: define the problem (III.A.1) → assess impact (III.A.2) → apply governance to design decisions (III.A.3) → identify and manage design risks (III.A.4) → document everything (III.A.5).

Why it matters for the exam: III.A.3 is the most frequently tested PI. It covers a long list of design-phase activities where governance applies — architecture selection, human oversight design, stakeholder engagement, metric thresholds. Any question about "should governance apply to X in the design phase" almost certainly cites III.A.3. III.A.4 is tested through risk hierarchy scenarios — the BOK expects you to know that governance applies a structured elimination → reduction → controls hierarchy before accepting residual risk.
Key precision points
III.A.2 timing: Impact assessment happens during design, not only at deployment. It covers harms to individuals, groups, organizations, and society.
III.A.4 tools: Probability/severity harms matrix, risk mitigation hierarchy, stakeholder mapping, use-case evaluation, benchmarking, pre-deployment pilots.
III.A.5 purpose: Exactly two stated purposes — to establish compliance and manage risks. Not "audit trail" or "regulatory submission."
III.B — Govern Data, Training & Testing

III.B covers everything that happens with data once design is approved — rights, provenance, training, and the full test suite. III.B.1 and III.B.2 deal with data rights and traceability before touching the model. III.B.3 through III.B.5 govern the training and testing process itself.

Why it matters for the exam: III.B.3's list of test types is a top exam target — candidates drop points by treating bias and interpretability testing as optional or regulatory add-ons. The BOK states them as required types alongside unit and performance tests. III.B.4 pairs with III.B.5: identifying an issue during testing is not enough — you must manage it and document the outcome.
Key precision points
III.B.1 lawful rights: "Assess and document lawful rights to collect and use data" — both elements required. Not just GDPR compliance; the BOK sets its own standard.
III.B.2 lineage vs quality: Lineage/provenance (III.B.2) is where data came from and how it moved. Quality/fit-for-purpose (III.B.1) is whether it's suitable. These are distinct requirements.
III.B.3 full test list: Unit · Integration · Validation · Performance · Security · Bias · Interpretability. Security and interpretability are frequently missed.
III.B.5 purpose: To validate results, establish compliance and manage risks — three stated purposes vs III.A.5's two.
III.C — Govern Release, Monitoring & Maintenance

III.C carries the highest exam weight of Domain III (8–10 questions). It governs the system from the moment before release through ongoing operation. The six PIs span readiness (III.C.1), ongoing monitoring (III.C.2), periodic deep assessment (III.C.3), incident governance (III.C.4 + III.C.5), and external transparency (III.C.6).

Why it matters for the exam: III.C.2 is the most common weak point — candidates confuse it with III.C.3. The distinction: III.C.2 is continuous and scheduled (everyday monitoring, regular retraining cadence); III.C.3 is periodic and structured (formal audits, red team exercises, threat modeling). III.C.4 and III.C.5 are frequently paired in scenario questions — you must identify the incident (III.C.4) AND conduct cross-functional root cause analysis (III.C.5). Passing an AI team's internal conclusion of "working as designed" is not sufficient for III.C.5.
Key precision points
III.C.2 vs III.C.3: III.C.2 = continuous + schedule-driven. III.C.3 = periodic + structured (audits, red teaming, threat modeling, security testing). Both are required; neither substitutes for the other.
III.C.5 root cause examples: Brittleness, lack of robustness, lack of quality data, insufficient testing, model drift, data drift. These terms appear verbatim on the exam.
III.C.6 recipients: Public disclosures include technical documentation and instructions for use to deployers — not just end users. Post-market monitoring plans are explicitly included.
Model card (III.C.1): Created at release readiness stage, not during design. Satisfying conformity requirements is paired with model card creation in the same PI.

Scenario Loan Approval AI — Full Lifecycle Governance Review ▾
Situation
A regional bank builds and deploys a machine learning system to approve or decline consumer loan applications. You are the AI governance lead reviewing governance coverage at each lifecycle stage. Six months post-deployment, three complaints arrive alleging systematic decline of qualified applicants from one postal district.
Design Stage — III.A findings
The design team defined the use case and built a probability/severity harms matrix, but the impact assessment addressed only credit risk — not demographic harms. Human oversight was designed as a second-review flag triggered only above a certain loan amount, leaving low-value applications fully automated. III.A.2 required the impact assessment to cover harms to individuals and groups; restricting scope to credit risk missed the demographic harm dimension. III.A.3 required human oversight to be embedded as a design principle, not a threshold exception.
Training and Testing Stage — III.B findings
The team sourced historical loan data from an acquired bank without verifying lawful rights of use under the new entity's policies. The test suite included unit and performance tests, but not bias tests. When validation accuracy was 3% lower than training accuracy, the team logged the gap but shipped anyway. III.B.1 required assessing and documenting lawful rights before using the acquired data. III.B.3 required bias testing as an explicit test type. III.B.4 required managing the accuracy gap issue — logging without action violated the "manage" obligation.
Post-Deployment Stage — III.C findings
Six months in, no monitoring schedule was in place — the team checked performance "when someone asked." The three complaints were forwarded to the product team and no incident record was created. When the governance lead convenes a cross-functional review, the AI team says the model "performed as trained." III.C.2 required a regular monitoring and retraining schedule from day one. III.C.4 required managing and documenting complaints as incidents. III.C.5 required cross-functional root cause analysis — "performing as trained" does not discharge this obligation when demographic harm has been flagged.
Outcome
Good governance would have caught the demographic impact gap at III.A.2, required bias testing at III.B.3, and triggered an immediate incident record and cross-functional review at III.C.4 and III.C.5. Each stage of Domain III provides a checkpoint — failing any one of them can compound into a post-deployment compliance crisis. The bank now faces regulatory scrutiny that a well-executed governance lifecycle would have surfaced and addressed internally.
Recall Quick Recall — 8 Cards ▾
?III.A.4 references a "risk mitigation hierarchy." What sequence does it imply?
Elimination first, then reduction, then controls — residual risk is accepted only after higher-order mitigations are considered.
?What does III.A.5 identify as the two purposes of design documentation?
To establish compliance and manage risks. Verbatim from the BOK — not "audit trail" or "regulatory reporting."
?What seven test types does III.B.3 specify?
Unit · Integration · Validation · Performance · Security · Bias · Interpretability. All seven are required — none are optional or purely regulatory add-ons.
?What does III.B.2 require, and how is it different from III.B.1?
III.B.2 requires establishing and documenting data lineage and provenance (where data came from and how it moved). III.B.1 requires assessing lawful rights and data quality/fitness — these are distinct obligations.
?What does III.C.1 require before releasing an AI system to production?
Assess readiness, create the model card, and satisfy conformity requirements. All three are in III.C.1 — the model card is a BOK requirement, not just a best practice.
?What is the precise distinction between III.C.2 and III.C.3?
III.C.2 = continuous monitoring with a regular schedule for maintenance, updates, and retraining. III.C.3 = periodic structured assessments: audits, red teaming, threat modeling, and security testing. Both are required independently.
?III.C.5 lists five factors explaining why incidents arise. What are they?
Brittleness · Lack of robustness · Lack of quality data · Insufficient testing · Model or data drift. These terms appear verbatim in the BOK and on the exam.
?What does III.C.6 require to be disclosed, and to whom?
Technical documentation, instructions for use to deployers, and post-market monitoring plans. Deployers are explicitly named recipients — not just end users or regulators.
