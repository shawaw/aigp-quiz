Concepts Key Concepts ▸
GDPR Data Protection Principles Applied to AI
Why it matters: Every AI system that processes personal data must satisfy all eight GDPR principles — not just the ones easy to implement. Examiners test whether you can apply these to training data, inference outputs, and model storage, not just raw databases.
Principle → AI implication
Lawfulness / Fairness / Transparency — AI training must have a valid legal basis; individuals must be told when AI processes their data and in what way
Purpose Limitation — Data collected for one purpose (e.g. HR records) cannot be repurposed to train a behavioural AI model without a compatible purpose analysis or fresh consent
Data Minimisation — Models should not ingest more personal data than necessary; synthetic data or aggregation should replace personal data where feasible
Accuracy — Stale training data can embed outdated facts about individuals; controllers must have processes to correct or retrain
Storage Limitation — Personal data embedded in model weights is still "processed" under GDPR; retention schedules must account for model lifecycle
Integrity & Confidentiality — Adversarial attacks, model inversion, and membership inference attacks are security risks the controller must address
Accountability — Controllers must demonstrate compliance (records of processing, DPIAs, audit trails)
Lawful Bases for AI Data Processing
Why it matters: Consent is often impractical at AI scale. Examiners probe whether you can select the correct basis and understand its limits — particularly for training data reuse.
Basis → AI use-case fit
Contract (Art. 6(1)(b)) — Processing necessary to perform a contract with the data subject. Works for personalisation that the user contracted for; does not extend to training new models
Legitimate Interests (Art. 6(1)(f)) — Must pass three-part test: purpose, necessity, balancing. Often used for fraud-detection AI and internal analytics; fails if the individual's interests or rights override
Legal Obligation (Art. 6(1)(c)) — AML/KYC AI in financial services; regulator-mandated monitoring
Consent (Art. 6(1)(a)) — Must be freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous. Withdrawable at any time — complicates model retraining pipelines. High bar; often overused
Public Task (Art. 6(1)(e)) — Government AI systems performing official functions (e.g., benefits eligibility scoring)
Vital Interests (Art. 6(1)(d)) — Emergency AI systems only; narrow
Automated Decision-Making & Profiling — GDPR Art. 22
Why it matters: Art. 22 is the most AI-specific provision in GDPR. Examiners frequently test the "solely automated" threshold, the three narrow exceptions, and what "meaningful human review" actually requires.
Art. 22(1) — the right — Data subjects have the right NOT to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing, including profiling, which produces legal or similarly significant effects
"Solely automated" — Human involvement that is rubber-stamping (reviewing a dashboard for 2 seconds without authority to override) does NOT break the "solely" threshold
Three exceptions (Art. 22(2)) — (a) necessary for contract; (b) authorised by EU/Member State law; (c) explicit consent. For (a) and (c): controller must implement suitable safeguards — right to obtain human review, express view, contest decision
Special category data — Automated decisions based on special category data require explicit consent or substantial public interest + suitable measures (Art. 22(4))
Meaningful human review — Reviewer must have authority and capability to override; review must be substantive, not ceremonial
Data Subject Rights in AI Context
Why it matters: Classic rights (access, erasure) create technical challenges when personal data is embedded in model weights. Know the tension, not just the right.
Right of Access (Art. 15) — Individuals can request what data is processed about them, including profiling logic and significance. AI systems must be able to surface this
Right to Erasure (Art. 17) — "Right to be forgotten" — tension with model weights that may have learned from that individual's data; machine unlearning is an emerging technical response
Right to Explanation (Art. 22(3) + Recitals 63, 71) — For Art. 22 decisions: right to obtain meaningful information about the logic involved, significance, and envisaged consequences. Not a full algorithmic audit right
Right to Object to Profiling (Art. 21) — For legitimate interests basis; controller must cease unless compelling grounds override
Data Portability (Art. 20) — Applies to data provided by the subject processed by automated means; relevant for personalised AI services
Special Category Data in AI Training
Why it matters: AI models easily infer special category attributes (health, race, religion, sexual orientation) even when not explicitly trained on them. This is a high-frequency exam trap.
Categories (Art. 9) — racial/ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data for identification, health data, sex life/orientation
Default prohibition + exceptions — Processing prohibited unless one of 10 Art. 9(2) conditions applies; key ones for AI: explicit consent, substantial public interest, health/social care, research with safeguards
Inferred special category data — Model outputs that reveal or predict special category attributes are subject to Art. 9 even if training data was ostensibly clean — a critical AI governance gap
Biometric data for identification — Facial recognition is special category; requires explicit consent or statutory authority in most EU jurisdictions
DPIA for AI Systems
Why it matters: AI systems almost always trigger DPIA requirements. Examiners test when a DPIA is mandatory and what it must cover — not just the definition.
Mandatory triggers (Art. 35) — Systematic and extensive evaluation of personal aspects including profiling with significant effects; large-scale processing of special category data; systematic monitoring of publicly accessible area
AI-specific triggers — Profiling, automated decision-making, large-scale training datasets, biometric processing, novel technology — all routinely found in AI systems
DPIA content minimum — Description of processing + purposes; necessity/proportionality assessment; risk assessment; measures to address risks
High residual risk — If DPIA shows high residual risk that cannot be mitigated, controller must consult supervisory authority before deploying
Privacy by Design & Default (Art. 25)
Why it matters: PbD is a proactive obligation that applies at the design phase — before deployment. Examiners test whether you understand it as a technical + organisational requirement, not a checkbox.
Privacy by Design — Data protection principles implemented from the start: use pseudonymisation, minimisation, access controls built into the AI architecture, not bolted on after
Privacy by Default — Default settings must be the most privacy-protective option; users must actively opt in to broader processing, not opt out
AI application — Federated learning, differential privacy, synthetic data generation, on-device processing are PbD-aligned technical choices

Scenario AI Hiring Screening Tool — GDPR Compliance Assessment ▸
Situation
A German logistics company deploys an AI system that screens CV data, public LinkedIn profiles, and psychometric test results to automatically shortlist or reject job applicants. HR managers receive a ranked list and typically accept the top-ranked candidates without review. The system was trained on historical hiring data including performance reviews and retention data.
Your Role
You are the AI governance professional advising on GDPR compliance before the system goes live.
Analysis
Art. 22 applies. Rejection = legal/significantly significant effect. Processing is "solely automated" — HR rubber-stamping without authority or genuine review does not break the solely-automated threshold. The company needs a valid exception: contract (pre-contract steps) is arguable, but requires implementing suitable safeguards — applicants must be able to request human review and contest the decision. II.A.4 Lawful basis for training data reuse is problematic. Historical employee performance data was collected under a different purpose (employment management), not recruiting AI training. Purpose limitation analysis is required. Legitimate interests may justify it, but the balancing test must be documented. II.A.3 Special category risk. Psychometric data may infer protected characteristics (health, disability, personality traits linked to neurodivergence). The system's outputs may constitute inferred special category data even if not explicitly labelled. Art. 9 prohibition applies; explicit consent or substantial public interest exception needed. II.A.6 DPIA mandatory. Systematic evaluation of personal aspects of employees/applicants + profiling with significant effects = mandatory Art. 35 trigger. If residual risk remains high after mitigation, prior consultation with the German supervisory authority (BfDI) required before deployment. II.A.7
Outcome / Recommendations
Do not deploy until: (1) DPIA completed; (2) Art. 22 safeguards implemented — genuine human review with authority to override, applicant notice, contest mechanism; (3) purpose limitation analysis for training data documented; (4) special category data audit of model inputs and outputs; (5) privacy notice updated to disclose AI use in hiring.
Recall Quick Recall — Click to Reveal ▸
What three exceptions permit Art. 22 automated decisions despite the general prohibition?▸
(a) Necessary for a contract with the data subject; (b) authorised by EU or Member State law; (c) explicit consent of the data subject. For (a) and (c), the controller must implement suitable safeguards including the right to obtain human intervention, express a view, and contest the decision.
Does a human reviewing an AI decision always break the "solely automated" threshold under Art. 22?▸
No. The human involvement must be substantive — the reviewer must have genuine authority and capability to override. Rubber-stamping or reviewing a dashboard without real oversight does not break the solely automated threshold.
An AI model is trained on customer purchase history collected under a contract basis. The company wants to use the same data to build a predictive churn model. What GDPR principle applies?▸
Purpose limitation (Art. 5(1)(b)). The company must assess whether churn prediction is compatible with the original purpose. If not compatible, a new lawful basis (e.g., legitimate interests with balancing test) is required before repurposing the data.
An AI model was not trained on health data, but its outputs reliably predict health conditions from behavioural patterns. Does Art. 9 apply?▸
Yes. Inferred special category data is still subject to Art. 9 protections. The prohibition on processing special category data applies to outputs that reveal or predict such attributes, not only to inputs explicitly labelled as special category.
Name three AI system characteristics that trigger a mandatory DPIA under Art. 35.▸
1. Systematic and extensive evaluation including profiling with significant effects on individuals. 2. Large-scale processing of special category data or criminal conviction data. 3. Systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas. AI systems frequently trigger all three simultaneously.
How does Privacy by Default differ from Privacy by Design under Art. 25?▸
Privacy by Design requires data protection measures to be built into the system architecture from the outset. Privacy by Default requires that the system's default settings process only the minimum personal data necessary — users must actively opt in to broader processing, not opt out.
Under GDPR, what must a data subject receive when contesting an Art. 22 automated decision?▸
Meaningful information about the logic involved in the automated decision, the significance and envisaged consequences of the processing (Art. 22(3) + Recitals 63, 71), plus the right to obtain human review and express their view. This is not a full algorithmic audit right but must be genuinely informative.
