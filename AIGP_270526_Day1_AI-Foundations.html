  • I.A — 1 Know the generally accepted definitions and types of AI.
  • I.A — 2 Identify the types of risks and harms posed by AI to individuals, groups, organizations and society (e.g., misalignment with objectives, ethics and bias risk, and complexity and scalability).
  • I.A — 3 Identify the unique characteristics of AI that require a comprehensive approach to governance (e.g., complexity, opacity, autonomy, speed and scale, potential for harm or misuse, data dependency, and probabilistic versus deterministic outputs).

Section 3 — Key Concepts ▾
3.1 Definitions and Types of AI ▾

The AIGP does not require deep technical knowledge of AI, but it does require precise use of terminology. Artificial intelligence refers broadly to systems that simulate human cognitive functions — reasoning, learning, problem-solving, and perception. For governance purposes, the distinction between AI types matters because different types carry different risk profiles and regulatory obligations.

Narrow AI (the dominant form today) performs specific tasks — fraud detection, image classification, credit scoring — within a defined scope. It cannot generalize beyond its training. General AI remains theoretical: a system that can reason and learn across any domain. Machine learning (ML) is the primary mechanism by which most modern AI systems learn patterns from data, without being explicitly programmed. Deep learning uses layered neural networks to process unstructured data like text, images, and audio.

Generative AI is the category most relevant to current governance challenges. These systems create new content (text, images, code, audio) rather than simply classifying inputs. They introduce distinct governance concerns: synthetic content, hallucinations, and IP risk. Agentic AI systems act autonomously toward goals across multi-step tasks — they do not just respond, they plan and execute. The AIGP BOK explicitly calls out both as governance frontiers.

Governance implication: The AI type determines the appropriate oversight mechanism. A narrow classifier needs performance monitoring; a generative model needs output review controls; an agentic system needs autonomous action limits and escalation protocols.

Precise AIGP terms

Narrow AI — AI designed and trained to perform one specific task or set of related tasks.
Machine learning — A subset of AI in which systems learn from data rather than following explicit instructions.
Generative AI — AI capable of producing novel content (text, images, audio, code) based on patterns in training data.
Agentic AI — AI systems that operate autonomously across multi-step tasks, making decisions to achieve a goal with limited human intervention.
3.2 AI Risks and Harms — Four Levels ▾

The BOK categorizes AI harms by who is affected. This taxonomy is directly examinable: questions will present a scenario and ask you to correctly identify the harm type and its governance implication. Three risk categories are explicitly listed in the BOK.

Misalignment with objectives occurs when an AI system optimizes for a measurable proxy rather than the actual intended outcome. A hiring algorithm trained to predict "success" using historical data may optimize for demographic attributes correlated with past hires rather than actual job performance — producing legally and ethically problematic results.

Ethics and bias risk is the risk that AI outputs reflect, amplify, or introduce discriminatory patterns. This can arise from training data (historical bias), model architecture (proxy variables), or deployment context (inappropriate application of a model outside its intended scope).

Complexity and scalability risk refers to the difficulty of predicting AI behavior at scale. A model that performs well in testing may behave unexpectedly when deployed to millions of users with diverse inputs. Scale also amplifies any embedded bias or error.

Governance implication: Risk identification at four levels (individual, group, organizational, societal) determines who must be consulted in a risk assessment and which mitigation strategies apply. Societal harms often require regulatory engagement; individual harms may trigger subject-rights obligations under privacy law.

Harm levels — AIGP taxonomy

Individual harm — Discrimination, privacy violation, financial loss, physical injury to a specific person.
Group harm — Systematic disadvantage to a demographic category or community.
Organizational harm — Reputational damage, legal liability, financial loss, operational failure for the deploying entity.
Societal harm — Erosion of democratic processes, public safety risks, concentration of power, environmental impact at scale.
3.3 Unique Characteristics Requiring Governance ▾

This is the most frequently examined concept in I.A. The BOK identifies seven characteristics that distinguish AI from conventional software and explain why generic IT governance is insufficient. You must be able to identify each, define it, and explain what governance mechanism it necessitates.

Complexity: Modern AI systems contain billions of parameters interacting in non-linear ways. No human can trace how a specific input becomes a specific output. This complexity undermines traditional audit approaches that assume human-legible logic.

Opacity (often called the "black box" problem): AI systems — especially deep learning — cannot easily explain their reasoning. In regulated contexts (credit, employment, healthcare), unexplainability may breach legal requirements for decision transparency.

Autonomy: AI systems act, learn, and adapt without direct human instruction for each decision. Agentic systems may take consequential actions without a human in the loop at the moment of action.

Speed and scale: AI decisions can be made millions of times per second. A flawed model deployed at scale can produce harmful outputs across millions of individuals before any human reviewer can intervene.

Potential for harm or misuse: AI can be deliberately weaponized (deepfakes, automated disinformation, adversarial attacks) or cause harm through unintended misuse even when designed benignly.

Data dependency: AI system quality is bounded by training data quality. Biased, incomplete, or stale data produces biased, incomplete, or outdated AI behavior. Governance of the AI therefore requires governance of the underlying data.

Probabilistic versus deterministic outputs: Unlike conventional software that produces the same output for the same input, AI outputs are probabilistic. The same input may produce different outputs under different conditions. This complicates testing, auditing, and accountability.

Governance implication: Each characteristic maps to a specific governance requirement. Opacity → explainability obligations. Autonomy → human oversight controls. Data dependency → data governance integration. Probabilistic outputs → ongoing monitoring rather than one-time testing.
3.4 Responsible AI Principles ▾

Responsible AI principles are the normative foundation of the AIGP program. They translate values into actionable governance requirements. The BOK lists six principles, each of which maps to concrete organizational practices. Note that "responsible AI" is the IAPP/AIGP term — do not use "ethical AI" or "trustworthy AI" interchangeably in exam answers unless the question uses that terminology.

Fairness: AI outputs should not discriminate unlawfully or cause unjustified differential treatment. Fairness is not simply the absence of bias — it requires active testing, monitoring, and remediation.

Safety and reliability: AI systems should perform as intended, consistently, even under adversarial or unexpected conditions. Safety failures may trigger product liability; reliability failures erode user trust and organizational credibility.

Privacy and security: AI systems that process personal data carry all data privacy obligations under applicable law, plus additional concerns around training data, inference attacks, and model inversion.

Transparency and explainability: Stakeholders — users, regulators, affected individuals — should be able to understand, to an appropriate degree, how an AI system makes decisions. Explainability is a technical property; transparency is a governance commitment.

Accountability: There must be identifiable humans or organizations responsible for AI system behavior and its consequences. Accountability requires clear role assignment, audit trails, and incident response procedures.

Human-centricity: AI should serve human well-being and dignity. This principle underlies requirements for human oversight, meaningful consent, and the avoidance of AI-enabled manipulation or coercion.

Governance implication: These principles are not aspirational — they are implementation requirements. Regulators (EU AI Act, Singapore's FEAT principles) translate them into specific obligations. Knowing the principle helps you trace its regulatory expression.

Key distinction

Explainability — A technical property of the AI model: the degree to which outputs can be traced to specific inputs or model features.
Transparency — A governance commitment: the organization discloses that AI is being used, how, and with what implications — even if the model itself is not fully explainable.

Section 5 — Worked Scenario ▾

Situation

A regional bank has deployed an AI credit-scoring model to automate loan decisions for applicants earning under SGD 80,000 annually. The model processes 12,000 applications per month and issues accept/reject decisions without human review. An internal audit finds that applicants from two specific postal codes — correlating with lower-income minority communities — are rejected at a rate 34% higher than the general population, despite similar financial profiles. The model vendor says the algorithm is proprietary and cannot provide an explanation of individual decisions.

Your role

As the bank's AI Governance Officer, you must advise the board on what governance failures this scenario reveals and what remediation is required before the system can continue operating.

Analysis

This scenario involves at least four of the unique AI characteristics from I.A — 3 and directly tests your ability to apply responsible AI principles from I.A — 4.

Opacity: The vendor's refusal to explain decisions is a governance failure, not merely a commercial inconvenience. In credit decisions, regulators (and in some jurisdictions, data subjects) have a right to explanation. An opaque model cannot be audited for compliance.

Data dependency and bias risk I.A — 2: The 34% differential rejection rate is a signal of potential proxy discrimination — the model may be using postal code (a lawful input) as a proxy for protected characteristics. This is bias risk materialized, causing harm at the group level.

Speed and scale: At 12,000 applications per month with no human review, the model can entrench discriminatory patterns across tens of thousands of individuals before any intervention.

Accountability gap: No human reviewer was in the loop. This violates the human-centricity and accountability principles under I.A — 4. In many jurisdictions, automated decisions on credit require either human oversight or the right to human review.

Outcome

The governance officer should recommend: (1) immediate suspension or human-review gate on all decisions pending investigation; (2) a bias audit — disaggregated outcome analysis by protected characteristics; (3) contractual renegotiation with the vendor to obtain explainability or switching to an interpretable model; (4) establishment of a human-on-the-loop review process for edge cases; (5) notification to affected applicants per applicable data subject rights obligations. This is not a technical fix — it is a governance remediation addressing failures in fairness, transparency, accountability, and human-centricity.

Section 6 — Quick Recall ▾

Click each question to reveal the answer. These are self-check prompts — not quiz questions.

What is the AIGP term for an AI system that acts autonomously across multi-step tasks to achieve a goal? reveal ▾
Agentic AI. The BOK explicitly distinguishes this from generative AI, noting it "plans and executes" rather than simply generating outputs. Governance of agentic AI requires autonomous action limits and escalation protocols.
What is the difference between "transparency" and "explainability" in AIGP terminology? reveal ▾
Explainability is a technical property of the model — whether outputs can be traced to inputs. Transparency is an organizational governance commitment — disclosing that AI is being used and how, regardless of whether the underlying model is fully explainable.
Name the seven unique AI characteristics that the BOK says require comprehensive governance. reveal ▾
Complexity, opacity, autonomy, speed and scale, potential for harm or misuse, data dependency, and probabilistic versus deterministic outputs. A useful mnemonic: CO-ASP-DP — Complexity, Opacity, Autonomy, Speed/Scale, Potential for harm, Data dependency, Probabilistic outputs.
An AI model rejects loan applications from a particular postal code at a significantly higher rate. Which BOK risk category does this represent, and at which harm level? reveal ▾
Ethics and bias risk (one of the three BOK risk categories under I.A — 2), manifesting at the group harm level. The postal code likely functions as a proxy for a protected characteristic, producing systematic disadvantage to a community.
What is the governance implication of probabilistic AI outputs? reveal ▾
Unlike deterministic software, probabilistic AI can produce different outputs for the same input. This means one-time testing is insufficient — governance requires continuous monitoring and statistical performance thresholds rather than pass/fail acceptance testing.
List the six responsible AI principles in the AIGP BOK. reveal ▾
Fairness, safety and reliability, privacy and security, transparency and explainability, accountability, and human-centricity. Remember them as the foundation from which all AI-specific regulatory obligations are derived.
Why is "data dependency" a governance concern rather than just a technical one? reveal ▾
Because AI quality is bounded by training data quality. Biased, incomplete, or stale data produces biased or unreliable model behavior. Governing the AI therefore requires governing the data that trained it — meaning data governance policies must precede and inform AI deployment decisions.
