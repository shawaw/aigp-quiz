BOKAll Week 1 Performance Indicators
I.A — Understand what AI is and why it needs governance
I.A.1 Know the generally accepted definitions and types of AI.
I.A.2 Identify the types of risks and harms posed by AI to individuals, groups, organizations and society (e.g., misalignment with objectives, ethics and bias risk, and complexity and scalability).
I.A.3 Identify the unique characteristics of AI that require a comprehensive approach to governance (e.g., complexity, opacity, autonomy, speed and scale, potential for harm or misuse, data dependency, and probabilistic versus deterministic outputs).
I.A.4 Identify and apply the common principles of responsible AI (e.g., fairness, safety and reliability, privacy and security, transparency and explainability, accountability and human-centricity).
I.B — Establish and communicate organizational expectations for AI governance
I.B.1 Define roles and responsibilities for AI governance stakeholders.
I.B.2 Establish cross-functional collaboration in the AI governance program (e.g., for efficacy and diversity of expertise and perspective).
I.B.3 Create and deliver a training and awareness program to all stakeholders on AI terminology, strategy and governance.
I.B.4 Differentiate approaches to AI governance based upon company size, maturity, industry, products and services, objectives and risk tolerance.
I.B.5 Identify differences among AI developers, providers, deployers and users from a governance perspective (e.g., with respect to responsibilities, opportunities and needs).
I.C — Establish policies and procedures to apply throughout the AI life cycle
I.C.1 Create and implement policies to ensure oversight and accountability across all AI life cycle stages (e.g., use case assessment, risk management, ethics by design, data acquisition and use, model and system development, training and testing, deployment and monitoring, documentation and reporting, and incident management).
I.C.2 Evaluate and update existing policies (e.g., data privacy, security, data governance and intellectual property) for AI.
I.C.3 Create, update and implement policies, assessments and contracts to manage third-party risk (e.g., procurement, supply chain, human resources and acceptable use).
II.A — Understand how existing data privacy laws apply to AI
II.A.1 Understand how transparency, choice, lawful basis and purpose limitation requirements apply to AI.
II.A.2 Understand how data minimization and privacy by design requirements apply to AI.
II.A.3 Understand how obligations on data controllers apply to AI (e.g., regarding privacy impact assessments, use of third-party processors, cross-border data transfers, data subject rights, automated decision making, incident management, breach notification and record keeping).
II.A.4 Understand the requirements that apply to sensitive or special categories of data (e.g., biometrics).
II.B — Understand how other types of existing laws apply to AI
II.B.1 Understand how intellectual property laws apply to AI (e.g., prohibiting or limiting use of data for AI training).
II.B.2 Understand how nondiscrimination laws apply to AI (e.g., in the employment, credit, lending, housing and insurance contexts).
II.B.3 Understand how consumer protection laws apply to AI (e.g., prohibiting unfair and deceptive acts or practices).
II.B.4 Understand how product liability laws apply to AI (e.g., prohibiting design and manufacturing defects).
II.C — Understand the main elements of AI-specific laws
II.C.1 Understand the risk classification framework for AI (e.g., prohibited/high/limited/minimal risk) and what systems/uses fall into each category.
II.C.2 Understand the key requirements around risk management, data governance, technical documentation, conformity/impact assessments and record keeping.
II.C.3 Understand the key requirements around human oversight, transparency and notification, and quality management.
II.C.4 Understand the distinct requirements for general-purpose AI models.
II.C.5 Understand the enforcement framework and penalties for noncompliance.
II.C.6 Understand the differences in requirements based on organizational context (e.g., providers, deployers, importers and distributors).
II.D — Understand the main industry standards and tools that apply to AI
II.D.1 Understand the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) principles, framework, policies and recommended practices for trustworthy AI.
II.D.2 Understand the NIST AI Risk Management Framework and Playbook (e.g., core functions, categories and subcategories).
II.D.3 Understand the core ISO AI standards (i.e., 22989, 42001 and 42005).
ConceptsWeek 1 Synthesis Themes▸
Why AI governance differs from standard IT governance
Exam trap: candidates conflate opacity with complexity. Opacity = inability to inspect the decision path. Complexity = many interacting components. Both are independent governance rationales under I.A.3.
The five AI characteristics that drive governance requirements
Opacity — The decision path cannot be inspected or explained. Creates accountability gaps in high-stakes decisions.
Autonomy — Acts without real-time human instruction. Governance must define intervention points before deployment, not reactively.
Speed and scale — Harms can propagate faster than human response. Monitoring cadence must match deployment velocity.
Probabilistic outputs — Same input can produce different outputs. Governance cannot rely on test-once-certify-forever.
Data dependency — Quality, provenance, and bias in training data determine system behaviour. Governance of the data is inseparable from governance of the model.
The actor map: developer → provider → deployer → user
Exam trap: a company can simultaneously be a provider (of its own model) and a deployer (using another vendor's model). Role determines obligation under the EU AI Act.
Role definitions (I.B.5 + II.C.6)
Developer — Designs and builds the AI system. Not a regulatory category in the EU AI Act — "provider" is the Act's term.
Provider — Places the AI system on the market. Bears the heaviest compliance burden: conformity assessment, technical documentation, CE marking, post-market monitoring.
Deployer — Uses a third-party AI system in a professional context. Must implement human oversight, monitor system, provide user information.
Importer — Brings non-EU AI into EU. Assumes provider obligations if no EU provider representative exists.
User — Natural person using AI in non-professional context. Fewest obligations; primary subject of transparency and fairness protections.
EU AI Act: four risk tiers and what triggers each
Exam trap: "limited risk" is NOT "low risk." Limited risk = specific transparency obligation only. Minimal risk = no obligations. High risk = full compliance regime. Prohibited = complete ban, no compliance pathway.
Risk tier triggers and obligations (II.C.1)
Prohibited — Social scoring by public authorities; subliminal manipulation; exploitation of vulnerabilities; most real-time biometric ID in public spaces. Complete ban. No conformity assessment pathway.
High risk — Critical infrastructure, education, employment, essential private services (credit, health insurance), biometric categorisation, law enforcement, migration, justice. Full obligations: risk management, technical documentation, conformity assessment, human oversight, post-market monitoring, incident reporting.
Limited risk — Chatbots interacting with humans, deepfakes, emotion recognition. Single obligation: disclose AI nature to user. No conformity assessment required.
Minimal risk — Majority of AI systems. No mandatory obligations. Voluntary codes of conduct encouraged.
GPAI models: a separate, parallel track
Exam trap: GPAI rules apply to the MODEL, not the downstream application. The open-source exemption is partial — copyright and technical documentation remain required.
GPAI obligation tiers (II.C.4)
All GPAI providers — Technical documentation, copyright law compliance, publish summary of training data used.
Systemic risk GPAI (>10²⁵ FLOPs training compute) — All basic obligations PLUS: adversarial testing, incident reporting to Commission, cybersecurity measures, full model evaluation report.
Open-source GPAI — Exempt from most obligations EXCEPT copyright compliance and technical documentation. Not a full exemption.
How existing law reaches AI: the II.A–II.B framework
Exam trap: GDPR does not require explicit consent as the only lawful basis. But automated decisions with legal effect require explicit consent or statutory grounds PLUS the right to human review.
Law-to-AI mapping
Data privacy (II.A) — Purpose limitation, data minimization, lawful basis, automated decision-making rights, sensitive data heightened threshold.
IP law (II.B.1) — Training on copyrighted data may infringe reproduction rights. No universal AI training exception exists.
Discrimination law (II.B.2) — Disparate impact is actionable without intent. Testing outcome distributions, not just model inputs, is required.
Consumer protection (II.B.3) — Deceptive AI outputs violate UDAP regardless of whether a human or AI generated the claim.
Product liability (II.B.4) — Design defect = fundamental design flaw causing systemic harm. AI harm from a known training flaw = design defect.
Frameworks compared: NIST, OECD, ISO
Exam trap: binding status matters. NIST AI RMF is voluntary. OECD principles are soft law. ISO 42001 is certifiable but not legally binding. None of the three create enforceable obligations on their own.
Framework properties (II.D)
OECD AI Principles — Non-binding soft law. Five principles: inclusive growth, human-centred values, transparency, robustness and safety, accountability. Endorsed by G20.
NIST AI RMF — Voluntary US framework. Four core functions: GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE. Not law. No international certification pathway.
ISO 22989 — AI concepts and terminology. Reference standard only.
ISO 42001 — Certifiable AI management system standard. Third-party audit pathway. Analogous to ISO 27001.
ISO 42005 — AI impact assessment guidance. Not certifiable. Supplementary to 42001.

ScenarioMulti-domain governance challenge: FinBridge credit AI▸
Situation
FinBridge, a Singapore-based fintech serving EU residents, deploys a third-party credit-scoring AI from a US vendor. The model ingests applicants' repayment history, social media activity, and employment data. Applicants from certain postcodes are rejected at twice the rate of others. The US vendor has no EU establishment. The vendor separately releases a foundation model under an Apache 2.0 open-source license.
Role
You are FinBridge's AI Governance Lead. The CEO wants to expand into France next quarter. Legal has flagged EU AI Act applicability. What must be resolved before launch?
Analysis
1. Risk classification (II.C.1): Credit scoring for individuals is explicitly listed as a high-risk AI use case. FinBridge cannot treat this as limited or minimal risk.

2. Provider vs deployer (II.C.6): The US vendor has no EU establishment. FinBridge, placing the system into service in the EU, may be deemed the provider and must satisfy provider-level conformity obligations.

3. Nondiscrimination (II.B.2): Postcode-based rejection disparities are a proxy discrimination risk. Disparate impact liability applies without intent. A statistical bias audit is required before EU deployment.

4. Automated decisions (II.A.3): Credit decisions are legally significant. EU applicants have the right to human review and explanation of logic.

5. Sensitive data (II.A.4): Social media data may inadvertently ingest sensitive data (political views, ethnicity markers). Heightened legal basis and controls required.

6. Third-party risk (I.C.3): The vendor contract must specify audit rights, liability allocation, technical documentation sharing, and incident notification obligations.

7. GPAI open-source (II.C.4): The vendor's open-source foundation model release does not eliminate copyright compliance and technical documentation obligations.
Outcome
France launch requires: bias audit, revised vendor contract with audit rights and provider-role clarity, subject rights mechanism, DPIA, and legal basis review for social media data. Launching without these exposes FinBridge to EU AI Act penalties (up to €15M or 3% global turnover) plus GDPR and discrimination law liability. I.C.3 · II.A.3 · II.A.4 · II.B.2 · II.C.1 · II.C.6
Recall8 high-yield exam traps from Week 1▸
Opacity vs complexity in AI governance — what is the distinction (I.A.3)?▾
Opacity = cannot inspect or explain the decision path. Complexity = many interacting components. Both are distinct governance rationales. Exam questions often use one to test whether you know it differs from the other.
What is the systemic risk threshold for a GPAI model under the EU AI Act?▾
Training compute exceeding 10²⁵ FLOPs. Triggers: adversarial testing, incident reporting to the Commission, cybersecurity measures, full model evaluation report.
Does open-source release exempt a GPAI provider from all EU AI Act obligations?▾
No — partial exemption only. Copyright law compliance and technical documentation remain mandatory. The exemption removes most, not all, GPAI obligations.
Limited risk vs minimal risk under the EU AI Act — what triggers each?▾
Limited risk = specific transparency obligation (e.g., chatbots must disclose AI nature; deepfakes must be labelled). Minimal risk = no specific obligation. "Limited" is not "low" — it has a named obligation.
A hiring AI rejects candidates from one ethnic group at statistically higher rates. No discriminatory intent. Is there liability?▾
Yes — II.B.2. Disparate impact doctrine imposes liability based on outcome distribution, not intent. The employer must demonstrate a compelling business justification or face liability.
What legal right is triggered when an AI makes a legally significant automated decision about an individual?▾
II.A.3 — the right to not be subject to solely automated decisions with significant effects, the right to meaningful information about the logic, and the right to obtain human review and contest the decision.
NIST AI RMF binding status and its four core functions?▾
Voluntary — not law. Four core functions: GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, MANAGE. No international certification pathway.
EU AI Act penalty tiers: prohibited practices vs high-risk AI violations?▾
Prohibited practices: up to €35M or 7% of global turnover (higher applies). High-risk AI violations: up to €15M or 3%. Misleading authorities: up to €7.5M or 1.5%.
