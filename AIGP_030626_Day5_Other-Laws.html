Other Laws & AI
BOK II.B — Performance Indicators

  2. II.B.2 Understand how nondiscrimination laws apply to AI (e.g., in the employment, credit, lending, housing and insurance contexts).

  4. II.B.4 Understand how product liability laws apply to AI (e.g., prohibiting design and manufacturing defects).
Concepts Key Concepts ▸
II.B.1 — Intellectual Property Laws▸

IP law intersects AI at two critical points: inputs (training data) and outputs (AI-generated content).

Why it matters: Organisations that train models on unlicensed copyrighted data face infringement liability. AI-generated outputs may also lack copyright protection if there is no human authorship.

Key Frameworks
Copyright (Training Data) — Using copyrighted works without licence or fair-use basis exposes the developer to infringement claims. Fair use is fact-specific; commercial AI training is contested.
Copyright (AI Outputs) — Many jurisdictions require human authorship for copyright protection. Purely AI-generated content may enter the public domain. Governance implication: document human creative contribution.
Patents — AI-assisted inventions: inventorship requires a human inventor in most jurisdictions. Governance teams must clarify patent strategy when AI contributes to invention.
Trade Secrets — Model weights, architectures, and training recipes may qualify as trade secrets if kept confidential. Vendor agreements must address confidentiality of AI assets.
II.B.2 — Nondiscrimination Laws▸

AI systems can produce discriminatory outcomes even without explicitly using protected characteristics — via proxy variables (e.g., ZIP code as a proxy for race, name as a proxy for gender).

Why it matters: Disparate impact liability does not require discriminatory intent. If an AI system produces statistically adverse outcomes for a protected class, the organisation may be liable regardless of design intent.

Contexts per BOK II.B.2
Employment — Title VII (US), EEOC guidance on AI hiring tools. Prohibited: selection criteria that disproportionately screen out protected classes without business necessity.
Credit & Lending — Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA), Fair Housing Act (FHA). AI credit-scoring models must not produce discriminatory denials. Adverse action notice obligations apply.
Housing — Fair Housing Act prohibits discriminatory AI-based listing recommendations, pricing algorithms, or tenant screening.
Insurance — State insurance regulations restrict proxy discrimination in AI-driven underwriting and pricing.
Disparate Impact — Legal theory: a facially neutral practice with disproportionate adverse impact on a protected class is unlawful unless justified by business necessity and no less discriminatory alternative exists.
II.B.3 — Consumer Protection Laws▸

Consumer protection law prohibits unfair and deceptive acts or practices (UDAP). In AI contexts, both prongs are active enforcement areas.

Why it matters: The FTC (US) and equivalent regulators globally have signalled AI as a UDAP priority. Deceptive AI disclosures and unfair AI-driven harm are enforcement targets.

FTC Act § 5 Analysis
Deceptive Acts — Material misrepresentation likely to mislead a reasonable consumer. AI examples: claiming human customer service agent when it is a bot; inflated efficacy claims for AI products; undisclosed AI-generated content in advertising.
Unfair Acts — Three-part test: (1) substantial consumer injury, (2) not outweighed by countervailing benefits, (3) not reasonably avoidable by consumers. AI examples: opaque AI pricing that exploits personal data; AI-driven dark patterns.
Governance response — Disclose AI's role in consumer interactions. Substantiate performance claims. Implement controls against dark patterns.
II.B.4 — Product Liability Laws▸

Product liability frameworks apply when AI systems cause harm. The key question: is the AI system a product subject to strict liability, or a service subject to negligence? Courts differ — governance teams must manage both.

Why it matters: AI-enabled products (medical devices, autonomous vehicles, industrial AI) face product liability exposure if they cause physical, financial, or other cognisable harm. Design and manufacturing defect theories are directly applicable.

Defect Types
Design Defect — The AI system's intended architecture or specification is unreasonably dangerous. Example: a triage AI designed to deprioritise based on a criterion that creates foreseeable patient harm.
Manufacturing Defect — The AI system deviates from its intended design due to flaws in the build or training process. Example: model trained on contaminated data produces systematically incorrect outputs.
Warning Defect — Failure to adequately warn users of known limitations or risks. Example: medical AI deployed without disclosure of accuracy limitations in out-of-distribution patient populations.
Strict vs. Negligence Liability — Strict liability: no fault required; defendant liable if product is defective. Negligence: plaintiff must show failure to exercise reasonable care. AI governance must address both tracks.

Scenario AI Hiring Tool — Multi-Law Exposure ▸
Situation
A fintech company licences a third-party AI hiring tool trained on proprietary candidate data scraped from LinkedIn and job boards without explicit licence. The tool uses ZIP code, name phonetics, and prior employer as selection features. It also tells candidates it is a "human screener" during the automated video interview. A rejected applicant's lawyer alleges the tool disproportionately excluded women and minority applicants from financial analyst roles.
Your Role
AI Governance Officer called to advise the legal and HR team on legal exposure and remediation.
Analysis
IP (II.B.1): Training on scraped data without licence creates copyright infringement exposure for the vendor, and potentially for the company as downstream user. The vendor agreement should have warranted clean training data.

Nondiscrimination (II.B.2): ZIP code and name phonetics are classic proxy variables for race and gender. Under EEOC guidance and ECOA, this triggers disparate impact analysis. The company cannot rely on vendor indemnity alone — deployer liability applies.

Consumer Protection (II.B.3): Representing an automated system as a "human screener" is a material misrepresentation — a deceptive act or practice under FTC Act § 5. Candidates could not make informed decisions about the process.

Product Liability (II.B.4): If the tool's reliance on proxy features was a design choice by the vendor, this is a design defect claim. If the training data contamination caused the discriminatory outcome, it is a manufacturing defect claim.
Outcome & BOK PIs Applied
Immediate actions: suspend the tool, conduct adverse impact analysis on all prior decisions, notify legal. Remediation: renegotiate vendor contract to require bias audits and data provenance warranties; implement disclosure to candidates; conduct pre-deployment bias testing for any replacement system.

Recall Quick Recall Flashcards ▸
What IP risk arises when an AI model is trained on scraped copyrighted data?▾
Copyright infringement — using copyrighted works without a licence or valid fair-use basis. Both the developer and potentially the deployer face exposure.
What is "disparate impact" and why is it critical for AI governance?▾
Disparate impact: a facially neutral AI practice that produces disproportionately adverse outcomes for a protected class. Liability can attach without discriminatory intent.
Name the three elements of the FTC "unfair act" test.▾
(1) Substantial consumer injury, (2) not outweighed by countervailing benefits, (3) not reasonably avoidable by consumers.
Distinguish design defect from manufacturing defect in an AI product liability claim.▾
Design defect: the intended architecture/specification is unreasonably dangerous. Manufacturing defect: the system deviates from its intended design due to flaws in the build or training process.
List the five contexts where nondiscrimination law applies to AI per BOK II.B.2.▾
Employment, credit, lending, housing, and insurance.
An AI chatbot claims to be a human advisor. Which law type is triggered and under which theory?▾
Consumer protection law — deceptive act or practice (FTC Act § 5). Material misrepresentation likely to mislead a reasonable consumer.
What IP protection can apply to an AI model's weights and training recipe if kept confidential?▾
Trade secret protection — provided the organisation takes reasonable steps to maintain confidentiality of the AI asset.
