Key Concepts

▼

AI Governance Models: Centralized, Decentralized, Federated

▼

Organizations structure AI governance in one of three primary models. A centralized model places all AI governance authority in a single function — typically a Chief AI Office or AI Centre of Excellence — that approves all AI initiatives, sets standards, and maintains enterprise-wide visibility. This maximizes consistency but can become a bottleneck in large organizations.

A decentralized model delegates governance authority to individual business units, each managing their own AI systems under general enterprise principles. Execution speed improves, but inconsistency across units creates compliance and reputational risk.

A federated (hybrid) model is the most commonly recommended structure: a central governance function sets enterprise standards, minimum requirements, and accountability frameworks, while business units implement and operationalize those standards locally. This balances consistency with agility.

Why it mattersExam scenarios test whether you can match the governance model to the organization's risk profile, size, and regulatory environment — and identify which model creates which failure modes.
Terms to know
Centre of Excellence (CoE)
A dedicated team that establishes AI best practices, standards, and tools — typically the nucleus of a centralized or federated model.
Business Unit AI Owner
The accountable individual within a BU responsible for an AI system's governance compliance throughout its lifecycle.

Key Roles in an AI Governance Structure

▼

AI governance requires deliberate assignment of roles. The Chief AI Officer (CAIO) holds executive accountability for AI strategy, policy, and responsible AI outcomes. The AI Ethics Officer translates ethical principles into operational requirements and monitors for value alignment.

The Data Protection Officer (DPO) becomes directly relevant when AI systems process personal data — focusing on lawfulness of processing, data minimization, and impact assessment obligations. The Chief Risk Officer integrates AI risk into the enterprise risk framework. At the operational layer, data stewards govern training data quality and provenance. AI product owners hold day-to-day accountability for deployed AI systems. Internal Audit provides independent assurance that controls operate as designed.

Why it mattersThe exam tests role-to-responsibility mapping in scenarios. The key distinction is between accountability (who is ultimately answerable) and responsibility (who does the work). These can sit with different roles.
Terms to know
Accountability
The obligation to explain and justify decisions — cannot be delegated. The CAIO or business owner typically holds accountability.
Responsibility
The obligation to carry out specific tasks — can be delegated to technical or operational staff.
RACI
Responsible / Accountable / Consulted / Informed — the standard matrix for mapping AI governance roles to decisions and actions.

AI Ethics Board / AI Review Committee

▼

An AI ethics board is a cross-functional oversight body that reviews AI initiatives — particularly high-risk ones — before deployment and during operation. Its mandate is to assess whether AI systems align with organizational values, ethical principles, legal requirements, and stakeholder interests.

Composition should be cross-functional: technology, legal/compliance, risk, business, HR, and ideally independent external members. The board reviews new AI deployments above a risk threshold, investigates AI incidents, approves waivers to policy, and reports to executive leadership or the board of directors. It operates through defined terms of reference — not an informal consultation but a formal governance mechanism with documented decisions.

Why it mattersThe AI ethics board is the organizational control that prevents high-risk AI from being deployed without adequate scrutiny. Exam questions often test whether you can distinguish a legitimate board structure from a rubber-stamp or advisory-only arrangement.

Integration with Existing Governance Frameworks

▼

AI governance does not operate in isolation. The three lines of defense model maps cleanly: first line (business units operating AI), second line (risk, compliance, and AI governance functions setting standards and oversight), third line (internal audit providing independent assurance).

Corporate governance integration means the board receives AI risk reporting as part of ERM. IT governance (COBIT/ITIL) provides the control infrastructure AI systems run on. Data governance ensures training data quality and appropriate use. Privacy governance and the DPO role intersect where AI processes personal data.

Why it mattersRegulators and auditors evaluate whether AI governance is embedded in existing frameworks or bolted on as an afterthought. Integration demonstrates maturity and prevents governance gaps between functions.
Three Lines of Defense applied to AI
First Line
Business unit owners and AI product teams — own and operate AI systems, responsible for day-to-day controls.
Second Line
Risk, compliance, AI governance, and legal — set standards, monitor compliance, provide oversight without owning the systems.
Third Line
Internal Audit — provides independent, objective assurance that first and second line controls are effective.

AI Policy Hierarchy: Policy → Standards → Procedures → Guidelines

▼

A policy is a high-level mandatory statement of intent approved by senior leadership. Example: “The organization will not deploy AI systems that make fully automated decisions with significant legal effects without human review.”

Standards translate policy into specific, mandatory technical or operational requirements. Procedures are step-by-step instructions for implementing standards. Guidelines are non-mandatory best practice recommendations where discretion is appropriate.

Ownership mirrors the hierarchy: policies owned by executive leadership or the board; standards by the AI governance or risk function; procedures by operational teams; guidelines by subject matter experts. Compliance is mandatory for policies and standards, voluntary for guidelines.

Why it mattersExam scenarios test whether you can identify which layer is being violated, who should own the remediation, and whether a gap is a policy failure, a standards gap, or a procedural breakdown.

Identifying Internal and External Stakeholders

▼

Internal stakeholders include executive leadership, business unit owners, data scientists and engineers, legal and compliance, HR, internal audit, and employees affected by AI-driven decisions. External stakeholders include customers and end users, regulators and supervisory authorities, third-party AI vendors and cloud providers, auditors, and affected communities.

Third-party vendor relationships introduce supply-chain governance obligations — the organization remains accountable for AI components it did not develop internally. Regulators may require notification, consultation, or approval before high-risk AI is deployed.

Why it mattersRegulators view inadequate stakeholder engagement as a governance failure. Exam questions test whether you identify the right stakeholders for a scenario and what engagement obligations follow.

Worked Scenario

▼
Situation
RegisBank, a mid-sized financial institution, has deployed AI systems across seven business units over three years. Each BU has built its own governance practices — some thorough, some minimal. A regulatory examination has flagged inconsistent AI risk documentation and no enterprise-level accountability framework. The Group CRO has been tasked with establishing a cohesive AI governance structure within six months.
Your Role
You are the newly appointed AI Governance Lead reporting to the CRO. You must recommend a governance model, propose a committee structure, and define how AI governance will integrate with RegisBank's existing risk and compliance frameworks.
Analysis
Step 1Choose the governance model. A centralized model adds a bottleneck across seven BUs. A decentralized model perpetuates the inconsistency the regulator flagged. The federated model — enterprise standards set centrally, BU implementation locally — is appropriate. I.B-4
Step 2Establish the accountability structure. CRO owns AI risk integration into ERM. A CAIO/Head of AI Governance (second line) sets enterprise AI standards. Each BU designates an AI Business Owner (first line). Internal Audit reviews annually (third line). I.B-1 I.B-2 I.B-5
Step 3Form an AI Review Committee. Cross-functional: AI Governance, Legal/Compliance, Risk, Business Representatives, DPO. Mandate: review and approve new high-risk AI deployments, investigate incidents, report quarterly to the board. Terms of reference must be documented. I.B-3
Step 4Build the policy hierarchy. Issue an enterprise AI Policy (board-approved), supporting AI Risk Standards (CRO-owned), BU-level implementation procedures, and guidance documents. I.B-6
Step 5Map stakeholders. Internal: BU owners, technology, HR, legal. External: banking regulators, external auditors, DPA (if AI processes customer personal data), and customers affected by AI-driven decisions. I.B-7
Outcome
A federated governance model is recommended. The AI Review Committee is chartered with documented terms of reference and cross-functional membership. An enterprise AI Policy and supporting standards are issued within 90 days. BU AI Business Owners are designated, creating accountability chains from individual systems to the board. Internal Audit schedules an AI governance effectiveness review in Year 1. The regulatory finding is remediated through documented accountability and consistent standards.

Quick Recall — Click to Reveal

▼
What is the key difference between accountability and responsibility in AI governance?tap to reveal
Accountability cannot be delegated — the accountable person must answer for outcomes. Responsibility can be delegated to those who carry out the work. The CAIO or business owner is accountable; data scientists and engineers are responsible.
In a federated AI governance model, what does the central function own versus the business units?tap to reveal
The central function owns enterprise-wide AI policies, minimum standards, and risk frameworks. Business units own implementation — they apply the standards to their specific AI systems and are accountable for local compliance. BUs cannot set standards below the enterprise minimum.
What three characteristics define a legitimate AI ethics board versus an advisory-only arrangement?tap to reveal
(1) Formal decision-making authority — it can approve, reject, or require modification of AI deployments. (2) Documented terms of reference — scope, mandate, composition, quorum, and escalation paths are written down. (3) Cross-functional and independent composition — includes external members and is not dominated by the function being reviewed.
Which tier of the policy hierarchy is mandatory versus discretionary?tap to reveal
Policies and standards are mandatory — non-compliance is a control failure. Procedures are also mandatory where they exist. Guidelines are discretionary — best practice recommendations that are not compliance requirements. Deviating from a standard requires a formal waiver; deviating from a guideline does not.
What is the DPO's specific role in AI governance?tap to reveal
The DPO's AI governance role is scoped to AI systems that process personal data: advising on lawfulness of processing, overseeing DPIAs, liaising with the DPA, and advising on data minimization. The DPO does not own broad AI ethics — that sits with the AI Ethics Officer or CAIO.
Name the three lines of defense in an AI governance context.tap to reveal
First line: AI system owners and business unit teams who operate AI — own systems and day-to-day controls. Second line: AI governance, risk, and compliance functions — set standards, provide oversight, challenge first line without owning systems. Third line: Internal Audit — independent assurance that first and second line controls are effective.
Why are third-party AI vendors considered stakeholders in the organization's governance framework?tap to reveal
Because accountability for AI outcomes does not transfer to the vendor. If an organization deploys a third-party AI model, it remains accountable for that system's impact and for regulatory compliance. Vendors are part of the AI supply chain and must be subject to due diligence, contractual governance obligations, and ongoing monitoring.
