I.C — Lifecycle Policies

Establish policies and procedures to apply throughout the AI life cycle · Exam weight: 6–8 questions
BOK Performance Indicators — I.C
  1. I.C.1 Create and implement policies to ensure oversight and accountability across all AI life cycle stages (e.g., use case assessment, risk management, ethics by design, data acquisition and use, model and system development, training and testing, deployment and monitoring, documentation and reporting, and incident management).
  2. I.C.2 Evaluate and update existing policies (e.g., data privacy, security, data governance and intellectual property) for AI.
  3. I.C.3 Create, update and implement policies, assessments and contracts to manage third-party risk (e.g., procurement, supply chain, human resources and acceptable use).
CONCEPTS Key Concepts ▶
AI Lifecycle Policy Coverage (I.C.1)

Governance policies must span every stage of the AI lifecycle — not just deployment. The BOK specifies nine stages: use case assessment, risk management, ethics by design, data acquisition and use, model and system development, training and testing, deployment and monitoring, documentation and reporting, and incident management.

Why it matters: A policy gap at any single stage creates a governance blind spot. The exam tests whether you know lifecycle coverage is end-to-end, not deployment-only. A policy framework that skips the use-case gate or ignores incident management fails I.C.1.

Key distinctions
Ethics by design — embedding fairness, safety and transparency requirements at the architecture stage, not post-hoc.
Use case assessment — the front-gate policy check before development begins: is this AI application appropriate, lawful, and aligned with organizational values?
Incident management — policies must define how AI-related harms, failures and near-misses are reported, investigated and remediated.
Adapting Existing Policies for AI (I.C.2)

Existing organizational policies — data privacy, cybersecurity, data governance, intellectual property — were written before AI. They must be evaluated and updated to address AI-specific considerations: training data rights, model outputs as IP, re-identification risks from inference, adversarial attack vectors, and automated decision-making obligations.

Why it matters: The exam will present scenarios where an existing policy is applied to an AI context. You must identify whether it requires updating — not just whether it "applies." Assuming coverage without updating violates I.C.2.

Policy update triggers
Data privacy → AI — automated decision-making, purpose limitation for training data, re-identification risk from model outputs.
IP policy → AI — ownership of AI-generated outputs, rights to use third-party training data, open-source model licensing.
Security policy → AI — adversarial attack vectors: prompt injection, model inversion, data poisoning.
Third-Party AI Risk Governance (I.C.3)

When AI involves third parties — vendors, cloud providers, data brokers, contractors — organizations must address risk through three instruments: policies (internal standards for vendor selection and engagement), assessments (diligence before and during relationships), and contracts (binding obligations on third parties).

Why it matters: The BOK scope covers four categories — procurement, supply chain, human resources, and acceptable use. Exam distractors often omit contractual obligations or limit scope to vendor procurement, ignoring HR (data labelers, contractors) and supply chain (upstream model providers).

Third-party risk categories (BOK I.C.3)
Procurement — vendor AI capabilities, model provenance, training data rights; governed through selection policies and contract terms.
Supply chain — dependencies on upstream model providers and data suppliers; risks propagate downstream if not contractually addressed.
Human resources — contractors and gig workers used for data labeling, RLHF, red-teaming; must have acceptable use and confidentiality obligations.
Acceptable use — policy defining what employees and contractors may and may not do with AI tools provided by or to the organization.

SCENARIO Worked Scenario ▶
Situation
FinCo is deploying an AI-driven credit scoring model using a third-party data provider. The CISO flags that the existing data governance policy does not mention AI training data. Legal notes that the vendor contract has no clause about model retraining using FinCo customer data. The risk team wants to run a bias test but there is no testing policy in place.
Role
You are the AI Governance lead. Identify which I.C obligations are unmet and recommend the remediation path before deployment proceeds.
Analysis
Three I.C obligations are unmet. First, the existing data governance policy has not been updated for AI — violating I.C.2, which requires evaluating and updating existing policies for AI contexts. Second, there is no testing policy covering bias — violating I.C.1, which requires policies across training and testing stages and ethics by design. Third, the vendor contract does not address data use for model retraining — violating I.C.3, which requires contracts to manage third-party risk including procurement and supply chain.

I.C.1 — Create and implement policies to ensure oversight and accountability across all AI life cycle stages (e.g., use case assessment, risk management, ethics by design, data acquisition and use, model and system development, training and testing, deployment and monitoring, documentation and reporting, and incident management).
I.C.2 — Evaluate and update existing policies (e.g., data privacy, security, data governance and intellectual property) for AI.
I.C.3 — Create, update and implement policies, assessments and contracts to manage third-party risk (e.g., procurement, supply chain, human resources and acceptable use).
Outcome
Deployment is paused. The data governance policy is updated to cover training data sourcing, quality standards, and purpose limitation. The vendor contract is amended to restrict customer data use to agreed purposes only and to include audit rights. A bias testing procedure is added to the AI development policy. The use case gate is formally documented. Deployment resumes with a monitoring schedule and incident management triggers defined.
RECALL Quick Recall Flashcards ▶
Name the nine AI lifecycle stages that I.C.1 requires policy coverage for. ▶
Use case assessment · Risk management · Ethics by design · Data acquisition and use · Model and system development · Training and testing · Deployment and monitoring · Documentation and reporting · Incident management.
What does I.C.2 require you to do when AI is introduced to an organization that already has a data privacy policy? ▶
Evaluate and update the existing policy for AI — not apply it unchanged. AI introduces new scenarios (training data rights, automated decisions, model-output re-identification) that a pre-AI policy does not address.
What three instruments must a third-party AI risk program deploy under I.C.3? ▶
Policies · Assessments · Contracts.
Name the four third-party risk categories in I.C.3. ▶
Procurement · Supply chain · Human resources · Acceptable use.
A company uses freelance data annotators for RLHF. Which I.C.3 category governs this relationship? ▶
Human resources risk — contractors must be bound by acceptable use and confidentiality obligations in contracts.
What does "ethics by design" mean in the context of I.C.1? ▶
Embedding ethical requirements (fairness, safety, transparency) at the model and system design stage — not applying them retrospectively after development.
Which I.C PI is violated if an organization has AI deployment policies but no policy for what happens when the AI causes harm post-deployment? ▶
I.C.1 — incident management is one of the nine required lifecycle stages for policy coverage.
