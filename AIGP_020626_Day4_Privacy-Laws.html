How Existing Privacy Laws Apply to AI

II.ABOK Performance Indicators
  1. II.A.1 Understand how transparency, choice, lawful basis and purpose limitation requirements apply to AI.
  2. II.A.2 Understand how data minimization and privacy by design requirements apply to AI.
  3. II.A.3 Understand how obligations on data controllers apply to AI (e.g., regarding privacy impact assessments, use of third-party processors, cross-border data transfers, data subject rights, automated decision making, incident management, breach notification and record keeping).
  4. II.A.4 Understand the requirements that apply to sensitive or special categories of data (e.g., biometrics).
ConceptsKey Concepts▸
1 · Transparency, Choice, Lawful Basis & Purpose Limitation▸

Transparency requires organizations to tell individuals when and how AI uses their personal data, including when AI is making decisions about them. Under GDPR and similar regimes, privacy notices must describe automated processing in plain language — not just bury it in boilerplate.

Choice means individuals must have genuine control, including the right to opt out of certain uses (especially profiling) and the right not to be subject to solely automated decisions with significant effects (GDPR Art. 22).

Lawful basis: every processing activity must rest on a legal ground — consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, or legitimate interests. AI-specific challenge: consent obtained years ago for a narrow purpose may not cover reuse in an AI model.

Purpose limitation: data collected for one purpose cannot be freely repurposed for AI training. A compatibility assessment is required — factors include link to original purpose, context, nature of data, consequences, and safeguards applied.

Why it matters for the exam: Questions frame scenarios where an organization repurposes customer data to train a model without re-obtaining consent or performing a compatibility assessment. The correct answer invokes purpose limitation, not just consent.
Precision vocabulary
Lawful basis — One of six GDPR Art. 6 grounds that legitimises personal data processing. Distinct from consent alone.
Purpose limitation — GDPR Art. 5(1)(b): data collected for specified, explicit, legitimate purposes may not be processed in a way incompatible with those purposes.
Art. 22 (GDPR) — Right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing that produces legal or similarly significant effects; includes right to human review, explanation, and contest.
2 · Data Minimization & Privacy by Design▸

Data minimization (GDPR Art. 5(1)(c)) requires that personal data be adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary for the purpose. The core tension with AI: ML models typically perform better with more data, creating pressure to collect broadly.

Governance responses include synthetic data generation, differential privacy, federated learning (model trains locally, only gradients shared), and strict data retention schedules.

Privacy by Design (PbD) — the principle that privacy must be embedded proactively into system architecture from the outset, not added as a compliance afterthought. Ann Cavoukian's seven foundational principles: proactive not reactive; privacy as default; embedded into design; full functionality; end-to-end security; visibility/transparency; respect for user privacy.

Applied to AI: PbD requires architecture decisions — selecting a model type that achieves the task with less personal data; implementing access controls at design stage; building in data expiry.

Exam trap: PbD is not just a policy commitment — it requires implementation in the technical architecture. Choosing a model that doesn't need personal data at all is PbD. A privacy notice explaining data use is transparency, not PbD.
Precision vocabulary
Privacy by Design — Seven-principle framework (Cavoukian) requiring privacy as default and embedded in system architecture end-to-end.
Federated learning — Training technique where the model updates on local devices; raw data never centralised — a PbD data minimization mechanism.
Differential privacy — Mathematical technique adding calibrated noise to outputs so individual data cannot be inferred; reduces re-identification risk in AI outputs.
3 · Data Controller Obligations Applied to AI▸

Privacy Impact Assessments (DPIAs): GDPR Art. 35 mandates a DPIA before processing that is "likely to result in a high risk" to individuals. AI systems almost always trigger this: systematic profiling, automated decision-making, large-scale processing, novel technology, processing of special category data. A DPIA is not a one-time exercise — it must be revisited when the system or its use changes.

Third-party processors: An organization using an AI SaaS vendor remains the data controller; the vendor is a processor. A Data Processing Agreement (DPA) is mandatory. Controller must verify vendor provides sufficient guarantees; processor cannot engage sub-processors without controller's authorization.

Cross-border transfers: Training data flowing outside the EEA requires a transfer mechanism — adequacy decision, Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs), Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs), or derogations. AI supply chains routinely span jurisdictions; this is a frequent compliance gap.

Data subject rights in AI context: Right of access (what data feeds the model?), right to erasure (technically difficult — unlearning is an active research area), right to portability, and the Art. 22 right to not be subject solely to automated decisions. Organizations must have processes to respond within statutory deadlines even when the underlying system is complex.

Breach notification: 72-hour notification to supervisory authority under GDPR. AI-specific breach types: model inversion attacks (extracting training data from model outputs), membership inference (determining whether a specific individual's data was in training set), data poisoning incidents.

Exam focus: The DPIA is the most tested item. Know the triggers, mandatory content (description of processing, necessity/proportionality assessment, risk assessment, mitigation measures), and who the DPO must consult.
Precision vocabulary
DPIA — Data Protection Impact Assessment; GDPR Art. 35 requirement before high-risk processing. Distinct from a generic risk assessment: must include consultation with DPO and, where residual risk remains high, the supervisory authority.
Data Processing Agreement (DPA) — Mandatory contract between controller and processor under GDPR Art. 28; defines scope, nature, purpose, type of data, obligations.
Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) — EU Commission-approved contract clauses enabling lawful cross-border data transfers where no adequacy decision exists.
Model inversion attack — Attack that queries a model to reconstruct training data; constitutes a personal data breach if personal data is exposed.
4 · Sensitive & Special Category Data in AI▸

GDPR Art. 9 creates a higher-protection tier for: racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data processed to uniquely identify a person, health data, sex life or sexual orientation. Processing is prohibited unless a specific Art. 9(2) exemption applies — typically explicit consent, employment law obligations, vital interests, or substantial public interest.

Biometrics in AI: Facial recognition systems that identify individuals process biometric data. Under the EU AI Act, real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces is prohibited (with narrow law-enforcement exceptions). Post-hoc identification systems face high-risk classification requirements. Voice biometrics and gait analysis fall within the same category.

Inference risk — the critical AI-specific issue: AI models can infer sensitive attributes from non-sensitive inputs. A model trained on purchase history can infer health conditions; a model trained on social media activity can infer religion or political views. Even if you never collect health data directly, your model's outputs may constitute processing of special category data. Governance implication: conduct inference risk assessments as part of design.

This is where the AIGP exam distinguishes candidates: the inference risk point. An organization claiming it doesn't process special category data because it doesn't collect it directly is wrong if its AI system infers or proxies those attributes.
Precision vocabulary
Biometric data — Personal data resulting from specific technical processing relating to physical, physiological or behavioural characteristics that allows unique identification of a natural person (GDPR Art. 4(14)).
Inference risk — The risk that an AI system derives or proxies sensitive attributes from non-sensitive inputs, effectively processing special category data without explicitly collecting it.
Explicit consent (Art. 9) — Higher standard than standard GDPR consent; must be a clear affirmative act specifically referencing the sensitive data processing.

ScenarioWorked Scenario — FinTech Credit Scoring Expansion▸
Situation
A FinTech lender operates an AI credit scoring model in Singapore, trained on historical loan applications, repayment history, device usage metadata, and social media activity scraped from public profiles. The company wants to expand to the EU. The model returns a binary approve/decline output with no explanation provided to applicants.
Your Role
AI Governance Professional advising the FinTech on EU launch readiness. You have been asked to identify the privacy law compliance gaps.
Analysis
Lawful basis and purpose limitation (II.A.1): Social media data was collected by third parties for entirely different purposes. Scraping and using it for credit scoring almost certainly fails purpose limitation. The FinTech must either exclude this data or obtain a compatible legal basis — which is unlikely available for scraped public profiles.

Data minimization and PbD (II.A.2): Device usage metadata is questionable for necessity. A PbD review should assess whether the model performance genuinely requires this feature category, or whether a less data-intensive architecture achieves acceptable accuracy.

DPIA and Art. 22 (II.A.3): The EU processing will require a DPIA — automated decision-making with legal/significant effects is a mandatory trigger. The binary output with no explanation violates Art. 22: applicants have the right to obtain an explanation, express their point of view, and request human review. The FinTech must build an explanation and human escalation mechanism before launch.

Inference risk (II.A.4): Social media and device usage data may enable inference of health status, religion, or ethnicity — all Art. 9 special categories. Even if the FinTech does not label these as special category processing, the inference risk means it may be. An inference risk assessment is mandatory.
Outcome
Recommended pre-launch actions: (1) Remove social media data from training set; (2) Complete DPIA before EU processing begins; (3) Build Art. 22-compliant human review and explanation process; (4) Conduct inference risk assessment on remaining features; (5) Establish SCCs for any data transfer from EU to Singapore for model processing.
Cited PIs: II.A.1 (lawful basis, purpose limitation) · II.A.2 (data minimization, PbD) · II.A.3 (DPIA, Art. 22, cross-border transfers) · II.A.4 (sensitive data inference)
RecallQuick Recall — Click to Reveal▸
Under GDPR Art. 22, when does the right to human review of an AI decision apply? ▾
When the decision is based solely on automated processing AND produces legal or similarly significant effects on the individual.
What triggers a mandatory DPIA under GDPR for AI systems? ▾
Systematic profiling; automated processing with significant effects; large-scale special category data; novel technology with high residual risk. AI systems almost always meet at least one trigger.
What is the core tension between data minimization and AI/ML? ▾
GDPR Art. 5(1)(c) requires collecting only adequate, relevant, and necessary data — but ML model performance typically improves with more and richer data.
What does purpose limitation mean for repurposing data for AI training? ▾
Data originally collected for another purpose requires a compatibility assessment before reuse for AI training. An incompatible purpose requires a fresh lawful basis.
Why can an organization not claim it avoids special category processing simply because it never collects health data directly? ▾
AI systems can infer sensitive attributes (health, religion, ethnicity) from non-sensitive inputs. The inference itself may constitute special category processing under GDPR Art. 9.
What distinguishes Privacy by Design from a privacy notice? ▾
A privacy notice is a transparency obligation. PbD requires privacy embedded proactively in system architecture — technical measures (e.g., data minimization, access controls) built in from the start, not described after the fact.
Name two AI-specific personal data breach types that would trigger the 72-hour GDPR notification obligation. ▾
Model inversion attack (querying the model to reconstruct training data) and membership inference attack (determining whether a specific individual's data was in the training set).
