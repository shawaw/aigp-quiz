Week 3 Review — Governing AI Deployment & Use
Covers IV.A Evaluate Deployment Factors · IV.B Assess the AI System · IV.C Govern Deployment & Use · June 21 2026
BOK Performance Indicators — Verbatim from BOK v2.1
IV.A Evaluate key factors and risks relevant to the decision to deploy the AI system (6–8 questions)
IV.A.1 Understand the context of the AI use case (e.g., business objectives, performance requirements, data availability, ethical considerations and workforce readiness).
IV.A.2 Understand the differences in AI model types (e.g., classic vs. generative, proprietary vs. open source, small vs. large, and language vs. multimodal capabilities).
IV.A.3 Understand the differences in AI deployment options (e.g., cloud vs. on-premise vs. edge, and using the AI model as is or with fine-tuning, retrieval augmented generation, agentic architectures, or other techniques to improve performance and fit).
IV.A.4 Perform or review an impact assessment on the selected AI system.
IV.B Perform key activities to assess the AI system (5–7 questions)
IV.B.1 Identify and evaluate key terms and risks in the vendor or licensing agreement.
IV.B.2 Identify and understand the risks and opportunities that are unique to a company deploying its own proprietary AI model (e.g., increased obligations and higher potential liability).
IV.C Govern the deployment and use of the AI system (9–11 questions)
IV.C.1 Apply the policies, procedures, best practices and ethical considerations to the deployment of an AI system (e.g., data governance, risk management, issue management, and user training).
IV.C.2 Conduct continuous monitoring of the AI model and system, and establish a regular schedule for maintenance, updates and retraining.
IV.C.3 Conduct periodic activities to assess the AI system's performance, reliability and safety (e.g., audits, red teaming, threat modeling and security testing).
IV.C.4 Document incidents, issues, risks and post-market monitoring plans.
IV.C.5 Forecast and reduce risks of secondary or unintended uses and downstream harms.
IV.C.6 Establish external communication plans.
IV.C.7 Create and implement a policy and controls to deactivate or localize an AI system as necessary (e.g., due to regulatory requirements or performance issues).
ConceptsKey Concepts by Competency▸
IV.A.1 — Understand the Context of the AI Use Case

Before any deployment decision, the governance team must evaluate five dimensions: business objectives, performance requirements, data availability, ethical considerations, and workforce readiness. Missing any one creates a governance gap downstream controls cannot fully remedy.

Exam angle: Distinguish use-case context evaluation (IV.A.1 — Understand the Context of the AI Use Case) from impact assessment (IV.A.4 — Perform or Review an Impact Assessment on the Selected AI System). Context evaluation is the prerequisite; impact assessment is the formal structured review that follows.
Key terms
Workforce readiness — Whether users are trained and operationally prepared to use the AI system correctly and safely before deployment.
Performance requirements — Defined thresholds for accuracy, latency, uptime, and reliability that the system must meet to fulfil the use case.
IV.A.2 — Understand the Differences in AI Model Types

Classic ML produces deterministic, auditable outputs. Generative AI produces probabilistic, high-variance outputs with opacity and hallucination risk. Proprietary models carry vendor dependency and limited auditability. Open source models allow inspection but introduce supply chain and fine-tuning risks. Multimodal models expand both capability and harm surface.

Exam angle: Model type drives proportionate governance controls. The key exam distinctions are proprietary vs. open source and classic vs. generative.
Key terms
Proprietary model — Vendor controls weights and architecture; limits deployer auditability.
Open source model — Publicly available weights; allows inspection but introduces supply chain and misuse risks.
Multimodal — Processes multiple data types (text, image, audio, video), compounding governance complexity.
IV.A.3 — Understand the Differences in AI Deployment Options

Cloud: scalable, third-party managed — raises data residency and sovereignty concerns. On-premise: full organizational control over data and infrastructure. Edge: local inference, reduces latency but limits monitoring. Beyond hosting: as-is (fastest), fine-tuning (requires training data rights), RAG (retrieval source integrity), agentic architectures (compounded human oversight risk).

Exam angle: Each option shifts a specific governance obligation. Fine-tuning triggers III.B.1 — Establish and Follow Data Governance Requirements. RAG requires retrieval source integrity. Agentic architectures amplify human oversight requirements under IV.C.1 — Apply Policies and Procedures to Deployment.
Key terms
RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation) — Supplements model responses with real-time knowledge base retrieval, without modifying model weights.
Agentic architecture — AI autonomously plans and executes multi-step tasks using tools and APIs, creating compounded governance risk.
Edge deployment — Inference on local devices; reduces latency but limits monitoring and update capability.
IV.A.4 / IV.B — Impact Assessment & System Assessment

IV.A.4 — Perform or review an impact assessment on the selected AI system is a deployment-stage requirement distinct from III.A.2 — Perform or Review an Impact Assessment on the AI System (design phase). The deployment context introduces new stakeholders, integration points, and constraints. IV.B.1 — Identify and evaluate key terms and risks in the vendor or licensing agreement: review indemnification, permitted use scope, audit rights, liability caps, IP ownership. IV.B.2 — Identify and understand the risks and opportunities that are unique to a company deploying its own proprietary AI model: increased obligations, higher potential liability — the organization becomes the AI provider in the regulatory sense.

Exam angle: Deploying proprietary AI increases, not reduces, governance burden — the organization absorbs obligations a vendor would otherwise hold.
Key terms
Permitted use scope — Contractual definition of allowed uses; exceeding it creates legal and contractual risk.
IP indemnification — Vendor commitment to cover costs if training data or outputs infringe third-party IP.
IV.C.1–2 — Deployment Policies & Continuous Monitoring

IV.C.1 — Apply the policies, procedures, best practices and ethical considerations to the deployment of an AI system: existing policies must be explicitly updated and applied — elements include data governance, risk management, issue management, and user training. IV.C.2 — Conduct continuous monitoring of the AI model and system, and establish a regular schedule for maintenance, updates and retraining: monitoring detects model drift and data drift. A documented retraining schedule is mandatory — ad-hoc retraining triggered only by incidents is insufficient.

Exam angle: IV.C.1 — Apply Policies to Deployment failures appear as scenarios where existing policies were not updated for the AI context. IV.C.2 — Conduct Continuous Monitoring failures appear as scenarios where drift was undetected due to no monitoring schedule.
Key terms
Model drift — Performance degradation as real-world data diverges from training data over time.
Data drift — Statistical shift in input data properties causing model outputs to become less reliable.
IV.C.3 — Conduct Periodic Activities to Assess the AI System

IV.C.3 — Conduct periodic activities to assess the AI system's performance, reliability and safety: red teaming (structured adversarial testing), threat modeling (mapping attack surfaces — prompt injection, model inversion, data poisoning), security testing, and audits. These are periodic, resource-intensive, and structured — distinct from the automated continuous monitoring of IV.C.2 — Conduct Continuous Monitoring.

Exam angle: Candidates conflate IV.C.2 — Conduct Continuous Monitoring (automated, ongoing) and IV.C.3 — Conduct Periodic Assessments (structured, scheduled). When a scenario describes a one-off structured review, IV.C.3 applies.
Key terms
Red teaming — Structured adversarial testing to elicit harmful, incorrect, or biased outputs.
Threat modeling — Systematic identification of adversarial attack surfaces targeting AI system integrity, availability, or confidentiality.
IV.C.4 — Document Incidents, Issues, Risks & Post-Market Monitoring Plans ⚠ WEAK

IV.C.4 — Document incidents, issues, risks and post-market monitoring plans requires two distinct deliverables: (1) a formal incident registry capturing what went wrong, when, and corrective actions taken; and (2) a post-market monitoring plan documenting what is monitored, at what frequency, and what thresholds trigger escalation. Without this documentation the organization cannot demonstrate compliance, identify recurring failures, or produce regulatory evidence.

Your weakest PI at 78% accuracy (Days 17–18). Key distinction: IV.C.4 — Document Incidents and Monitoring Plans covers what is RECORDED; IV.C.2 — Conduct Continuous Monitoring covers what is DONE operationally; IV.C.3 — Conduct Periodic Assessments covers structured, scheduled assessment activities. When a scenario describes evidence, records, or documentation, IV.C.4 applies.
Key terms
Post-market monitoring plan — Structured document defining what is monitored, at what frequency, and what actions threshold breaches trigger.
Incident registry — Formal log of AI-related incidents, near-misses, and issues including root cause and corrective actions.
IV.C.5 — Forecast & Reduce Risks of Secondary or Unintended Uses ⚠ WEAK

IV.C.5 — Forecast and reduce risks of secondary or unintended uses and downstream harms requires proactive anticipation of uses differing from the designed purpose — not reactive response after harm materializes. Controls include acceptable use policies, API access restrictions, contractual prohibitions on downstream resale, and impact assessments for each significant use expansion.

Your second weakest PI at 78% accuracy (Days 17–18). When a scenario describes unanticipated use by internal users or downstream third parties, IV.C.5 — Forecast Secondary and Downstream Harms applies. This is distinct from IV.C.4 — Document Incidents (which records harms after they occur).
Key terms
Secondary use — Application of an AI system for a purpose other than its designed and validated use case.
Downstream harm — Harm to individuals not directly served by the deployer, caused by AI outputs propagating through third-party systems.
IV.C.6–7 — External Communications & Deactivation Controls

IV.C.6 — Establish external communication plans: stakeholder communication strategies must be prepared before incidents occur — covering affected individuals, regulators, and public disclosures. IV.C.7 — Create and implement a policy and controls to deactivate or localize an AI system as necessary: covers (1) regulatory-ordered shutdown or geographic localization (data sovereignty), and (2) performance-triggered deactivation when outputs become unsafe. Both require documented protocols established in advance.

Exam angle: IV.C.6 — Establish External Communication Plans: no plan before the incident = IV.C.6 gap. IV.C.7 — Create Deactivation Controls: distinguish deactivation (full stop) from localization (geographic restriction).
Key terms
Localization — Restricting AI processing to a specific geographic region to comply with data sovereignty or regulatory requirements.
Deactivation controls — Documented policies and technical mechanisms for safely taking an AI system offline, fully or in a specific jurisdiction.

ScenarioDeployer Governance Failure — End-to-End▸
Situation
A regional insurer deploys a third-party cloud-based AI model to automate claims triage. The contract was signed by procurement without governance review. No impact assessment was conducted at deployment. Six months in, claims officers begin using the AI's outputs to set reserve amounts — a use case the model was not designed or validated for. Performance degrades without detection. When a regulator audits, the insurer cannot produce an incident log, monitoring plan, or external communication protocol.
Role
You are the incoming AI Governance Officer asked to conduct a gap assessment and remediation plan before the regulator's follow-up audit in 90 days.
Analysis
Gap 1: Vendor contract never reviewed — violating IV.B.1 — Identify and evaluate key terms and risks in the vendor or licensing agreement.

Gap 2: No deployment-stage impact assessment — violating IV.A.4 — Perform or review an impact assessment on the selected AI system.

Gap 3: Reserve-setting is a secondary use outside validated design — violating IV.C.5 — Forecast and reduce risks of secondary or unintended uses and downstream harms.

Gap 4: No incident log or post-market monitoring plan — violating IV.C.4 — Document incidents, issues, risks and post-market monitoring plans.

Gap 5: No external communication protocol — violating IV.C.6 — Establish external communication plans.
Outcome
The insurer presents a documented remediation roadmap anchored to specific BOK PIs, a retrospective incident registry, and a commitment to halt secondary use pending re-assessment. Mapping each gap to an exact PI — rather than offering general process improvements — is what demonstrates governance maturity to the regulator.
RecallQuick Recall — Click to Reveal▸
IV.A.1 — Understand the Context of the AI Use Case: what are the five required dimensions? ▾
Business objectives, performance requirements, data availability, ethical considerations, and workforce readiness.
What distinguishes IV.A.4 — Perform or Review an Impact Assessment on the Selected AI System from III.A.2 — Perform or Review an Impact Assessment on the AI System? ▾
IV.A.4 is a deployment-stage requirement applied to the actual system in its deployment context. III.A.2 occurs during design and build. The deployment context introduces new stakeholders and constraints not present at design time.
What does IV.B.2 — Identify and Understand the Risks Unique to Deploying a Proprietary AI Model say about obligations compared to vendor deployment? ▾
Deploying a proprietary model carries increased obligations and higher potential liability — the organization becomes the AI provider in the regulatory sense, absorbing obligations a vendor would otherwise hold.
How do IV.C.2 — Conduct Continuous Monitoring and IV.C.3 — Conduct Periodic Activities to Assess the AI System differ? ▾
IV.C.2 — Conduct Continuous Monitoring is automated and ongoing, detecting drift in real time. IV.C.3 — Conduct Periodic Activities to Assess the AI System (audits, red teaming, threat modeling, security testing) is structured, scheduled, and resource-intensive. One-off structured reviews = IV.C.3; continuous automated monitoring = IV.C.2.
What does IV.C.4 — Document Incidents, Issues, Risks and Post-Market Monitoring Plans require beyond a monitoring dashboard? ▾
A formal incident registry (what went wrong, when, corrective action) and a post-market monitoring plan (what is monitored, at what frequency, what thresholds trigger escalation). A dashboard shows current state; IV.C.4 — Document Incidents and Monitoring Plans requires durable documentary evidence for compliance and regulatory review.
IV.C.5 — Forecast and Reduce Risks of Secondary or Unintended Uses and Downstream Harms: what scenario pattern triggers this PI on the exam? ▾
Any scenario where an AI system is used outside its validated use case — by internal users repurposing it, or by downstream third parties applying its outputs in new contexts. IV.C.5 requires proactive anticipation before harm occurs, not reactive response after.
What are the two distinct scenarios covered by IV.C.7 — Create and Implement a Policy and Controls to Deactivate or Localize an AI System? ▾
(1) Regulatory-ordered localization — restricting processing to a specific jurisdiction to comply with data sovereignty law. (2) Performance-triggered deactivation — taking the system offline because outputs have become unsafe or unreliable. Both require pre-established documented protocols.
