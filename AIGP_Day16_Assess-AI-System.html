IV.B — Perform Key Activities to Assess the AI System
Estimated reading time: 20 min · Vendor contracts + Proprietary AI risk
BOK Performance Indicators — IV.B (verbatim)
  • IV.B.1 Identify and evaluate key terms and risks in the vendor or licensing agreement.
  • IV.B.2 Identify and understand the risks and opportunities that are unique to a company deploying its own proprietary AI model (e.g., increased obligations and higher potential liability).
Concepts Key Concepts ▾
1. Vendor / Third-Party AI Contract Assessment

When a company deploys AI built by a third party, its primary governance lever is the contract. Once signed, the deployer is responsible for AI behaviour in its environment regardless of who wrote the code. The contract is where risks get allocated — or left unallocated, which defaults to the deployer bearing them.

Why it matters on the exam: IV.B.1 is tested through scenario questions where a deployer is harmed by a gap in their contract — missing audit rights, uncapped liability, silent model updates. You need to spot which specific term was missing and why it mattered.

Eight Terms to Know
Liability cap The ceiling on financial damages the vendor will pay — typically set as a multiple of subscription fees and almost always far below real harm potential. Negotiate this up, and push to exclude consequential damages waivers for high-stakes use cases.
Indemnification Who defends and pays if a third party (e.g., a harmed customer) sues. Watch for carve-outs triggered by your own configuration choices — if you customise the system, the carve-out may void coverage.
IP ownership Who owns fine-tuned weights, output embeddings, or custom models created on the vendor's infrastructure. Standard contracts often assign derivative models back to the vendor. Negotiate this explicitly before deploying proprietary data.
Data usage rights Whether the vendor may use your inputs and outputs to train future versions. Conflicts with data minimisation, purpose limitation, and PDPA/GDPR requirements when the data contains personal information.
Subprocessor disclosure The right to be notified of, and approve, downstream parties who access your data during inference. Critical for cross-border data transfer obligations and regulatory accountability chains.
Audit and explainability rights Contractual entitlement to inspect model documentation, security controls, and decision logic. Without this, responding to a regulator's inquiry about AI decisions is nearly impossible.
Change notification / model versioning Requirement for the vendor to provide advance notice before pushing model updates that could alter system behaviour. Silent updates can change risk profiles without warning.
Exit and portability rights The right to export data, models, and decision histories at contract termination. Includes change-of-control clauses that allow immediate exit if the vendor is acquired by a competitor. Vendor lock-in is a governance risk, not just a commercial one.
2. Proprietary AI — Risk and Opportunity Profile

When a company builds and deploys its own AI system, it occupies both the provider and deployer roles simultaneously under most AI governance frameworks. This dual role triggers the full stack of obligations — technical documentation, conformity assessments, bias testing, risk management — that a pure deployer of third-party AI can partially offset against vendor documentation.

Why it matters on the exam: IV.B.2 is almost always tested through a "build vs. buy" comparison. You need to articulate what changes — specifically increased obligations and higher potential liability — and also name the genuine upside: IP ownership and competitive differentiation. Both sides of the ledger matter.

Key Definitions
AI provider (regulatory definition) The entity that develops an AI system or has it developed and places it on the market or into service — either externally or for internal use. A company that builds its own AI for internal deployment qualifies as a provider under frameworks such as the EU AI Act.
AI deployer (regulatory definition) The entity that puts an AI system into use within a professional context. A company building and using its own AI is both provider and deployer — no role separation exists to distribute accountability.
Higher potential liability No third-party vendor exists to absorb any fraction of claims through a contract. Every design decision, training dataset choice, and performance failure is the company's own.
Increased obligations As provider, the company must create and maintain its own technical documentation, perform its own conformity assessment, implement its own risk management system, and supply instructions for use.
IP ownership (opportunity) The model, training data, and all derivative assets belong to the company. This prevents vendor lock-in, protects the competitive moat, and enables licensing or commercialisation as a future revenue stream.
3. Dual-Role Accountability in Practice

The governance implication of being both provider and deployer is not abstract. It means the company must staff and fund the full provider compliance programme in addition to its deployer monitoring obligations. There is no vendor to call when a regulator asks for the model card, the training data assessment, or the risk management log.

Why it matters on the exam: Questions on this concept often present a "peer company uses a vendor" comparison to highlight what the proprietary builder must do that the peer does not. The correct answer identifies the provider-level obligation that the vendor would otherwise supply.

Provider Obligations That Transfer Inward
Technical documentation Design rationale, training data description, architecture, intended purpose, performance metrics, and known limitations — all must be created and maintained internally.
Conformity / impact assessment The company conducts its own pre-deployment assessment rather than relying on a vendor's certification. For high-risk AI, this involves demonstrating compliance with all applicable requirements before the system goes live.
Post-market monitoring Ongoing performance and safety tracking, incident logging, and reporting — typically shared with vendors in third-party deployments but entirely owned internally with proprietary systems.

Scenario Worked Scenario — Fintech AI Governance Assessment ▾
Situation
A Singapore-based fintech is building an AI-powered credit scoring system. The board is deciding between two options: (A) license a credit scoring model from an established vendor, or (B) build a proprietary model using internal customer transaction data. The AI governance officer must brief the board on the governance implications of each path before the decision is made.
Role
AI Governance Officer conducting a pre-deployment assessment and advising the board.
Analysis — Option A: Third-Party Vendor
The governance officer reviews the vendor's standard contract and identifies four gaps: (1) the liability cap is set at three months of fees — far below the potential regulatory fine for a discriminatory lending decision; (2) there are no audit rights over the model's decision logic; (3) the vendor reserves the right to use customer transaction data for model improvement; (4) there is no change notification clause. The officer recommends negotiating all four terms before signing. IV.B.1 — Identify and evaluate key terms and risks in the vendor or licensing agreement.
Analysis — Option B: Proprietary Build
The proprietary path gives the company full IP ownership of the credit scoring model — a competitive moat in a market where peers use the same vendor API. However, the company becomes the AI provider under applicable regulations, triggering obligations to create technical documentation, conduct its own conformity assessment, implement a risk management system, and operate post-market monitoring entirely in-house. There is no vendor to share liability if the model discriminates against a protected class. The governance officer quantifies the additional compliance investment required before the board can compare it against the IP opportunity. IV.B.2 — Identify and understand the risks and opportunities that are unique to a company deploying its own proprietary AI model (e.g., increased obligations and higher potential liability).
Outcome
The board chooses Option B with governance conditions: a dedicated AI risk function, a formal technical documentation programme, and a legal review of provider obligations under the applicable AI framework. The governance officer ensures the board understands that IP ownership is the upside, and sole accountability is the cost — both must be accepted explicitly before proceeding.
Recall Quick Recall — Click to Reveal ▾
What does a liability cap in a vendor AI contract limit — and why is it a key IV.B.1 risk?▾
The maximum financial compensation available from the vendor if the AI system causes harm. Caps are typically set at a multiple of subscription fees — often far below the actual damage potential. Negotiating the cap upward and preserving consequential damages is a core contract risk management task.
What is an indemnification carve-out in an AI vendor contract, and when does it expose the deployer?▾
A provision removing vendor indemnification coverage for claims arising from the deployer's own configuration choices. If you customise the system beyond vendor defaults and a third party is harmed, the carve-out means the deployer bears the full defence and damages with no vendor coverage.
Why does deploying a proprietary AI model create "higher potential liability" under IV.B.2?▾
No third-party vendor exists to absorb any fraction of claims through contractual liability sharing. The company designed the model, selected the training data, and made every architecture decision — it is the primary accountable party for all governance failures with no vendor indemnification to fall back on.
What is a change-of-control clause in an AI vendor contract?▾
A provision permitting the deploying company to terminate immediately if the vendor is acquired — particularly by a competitor. Without it, a 12-month notice period may give a competitor access to AI usage data and query patterns.
What are audit and explainability rights in a vendor AI contract, and why are they essential?▾
Contractual entitlements to inspect the vendor's model documentation, security controls, and decision logic. Without them, the deploying organisation cannot respond to regulatory inquiries about AI decisions or investigate incidents where model behaviour is disputed.
What is the primary opportunity of proprietary AI model ownership under IV.B.2?▾
Full IP ownership of the model and all training data — a competitive moat that vendor-licensed peers cannot replicate, eliminating vendor lock-in and retaining the option to commercialise or license the model in the future.
Under AI-specific legislation, when is a company both AI provider and AI deployer simultaneously?▾
When it develops an AI system internally and deploys it in its own operations or to end users — triggering the full set of provider obligations (technical documentation, conformity assessment, risk management, post-market monitoring) on top of deployer obligations.
What does a model versioning clause require a vendor to do?▾
Provide advance notice and version tracking for any model updates that could alter system behaviour. Without it, a vendor can silently push updates that change risk-scoring thresholds or model capabilities, invalidating prior compliance assessments.
