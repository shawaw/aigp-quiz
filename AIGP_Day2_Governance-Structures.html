Governance Structures
Domain I · Competency I.B · Exam weight: 5–7 questions

  2. I.B.2 Establish cross-functional collaboration in the AI governance program (e.g., for efficacy and diversity of expertise and perspective).
  3. I.B.3 Create and deliver a training and awareness program to all stakeholders on AI terminology, strategy and governance.

Concepts Key Concepts ›
AI Governance Roles & Responsibilities

A functioning governance program requires clearly assigned accountability. Common roles include a Chief AI Officer (CAIO) or AI Governance Lead, an AI Ethics/Review Board, and domain liaisons from Legal, Risk, Privacy, IT Security, HR and Business Units. Governance roles must be defined, not assumed — ambiguous ownership is the most common failure mode in early-stage programs.

Exam angle: Questions test whether you can assign the right role to a scenario action — e.g., who is accountable for a bias incident versus who operationalises the fix.
Key terms
CAIO — Senior executive accountable for enterprise AI strategy, governance and risk posture. Not interchangeable with CTO or CISO.
AI Review Board — Cross-functional body that approves high-risk AI use cases; distinct from the team that builds the system.
Three Lines of Defence (AI) — (1) Business/AI team owns day-to-day controls; (2) Risk/Compliance provides oversight; (3) Internal Audit provides independent assurance.
Cross-functional Collaboration

No single function owns AI governance. Effective programs embed governance across Legal, Privacy, Risk, Procurement, HR and product teams. The BOK specifies two rationales: efficacy (catching more risk vectors) and diversity of expertise and perspective. Cross-functional structures include steering committees, centres of excellence (CoE), or federated models with a coordinating hub.

Exam angle: Memorise the exact BOK phrasing — "efficacy and diversity of expertise and perspective." Distractors often use plausible alternatives like "efficiency" or "speed."
Key terms
Centre of Excellence (CoE) — Centralised expertise hub that sets standards and advises, but does not own every decision.
Federated governance — Embedded governance capacity in each business unit, coordinated by a central function. Suits large, diverse organisations.
Training & Awareness Programs

Training must reach all stakeholders — not just data scientists. The BOK specifies three content areas: AI terminology, strategy and governance. Audience segmentation matters: executives need strategic framing; engineers need technical standards; business users need acceptable use and escalation paths. A common gap is training that reaches technical staff but misses procurement, HR or senior leadership.

Exam angle: Questions often identify what is missing from a described program — look for incomplete audience coverage or missing content areas (terminology, strategy, or governance).
Key terms
Role-based training — Different depth and framing for different job functions; not one-size-fits-all.
Awareness vs. training — Awareness is broad and cultural; training is formal, assessed and role-specific. Both are required.
Differentiated Governance Approaches

There is no universal governance template. The BOK requires practitioners to calibrate based on company size, maturity, industry, products and services, objectives and risk tolerance. A startup with a narrow, low-risk use case needs a lean, proportionate program. A systemically important financial institution deploying AI in credit decisioning requires formal board oversight, dedicated risk functions, and regulatory engagement.

Exam angle: Scenarios describe a company context and ask what governance model is most appropriate. Match the approach to the context; never apply a single ideal model universally.
Calibration factors (I.B.4)
Size — Smaller organisations may combine roles; larger ones need formal separation of duties.
Maturity — Early-stage programs prioritise policy foundations; mature programs focus on continuous improvement and audit.
Risk tolerance — Conservative tolerance (healthcare, finance) requires pre-approval gates; higher tolerance allows post-deployment review.
AI Role Taxonomy: Developer · Provider · Deployer · User

The BOK distinguishes four roles carrying distinct governance responsibilities. The same organisation can occupy multiple roles simultaneously — e.g., a bank that builds its own model is both developer and deployer. Identifying the applicable role in a supply chain scenario is consistently tested.

Exam angle: Expect 2–3 questions presenting a supply chain and asking which role a described organisation occupies, and what its specific obligation is.
Role definitions
Developer — Creates/trains the AI model. Owns design choices, training data quality, bias controls, technical documentation.
Provider — Makes the AI system available to others. Responsible for conformity, instructions for use to deployers, post-market monitoring.
Deployer — Integrates and operates the AI system in a specific context. Responsible for use-case fit, human oversight, user notification, incident management.
User — Individuals who interact with or are subject to the AI system. May hold rights (explanation, appeal) but limited direct governance obligations.

Scenario Worked Scenario — Credit Scoring AI at a Mid-Size Bank ›
Situation
A mid-size retail bank is deploying a third-party AI credit-scoring model sourced from a fintech vendor. The bank has a small data team, a compliance function, and no dedicated AI governance role. The vendor provides an API, minimal documentation, and a standard commercial contract. The bank's CISO has been asked to oversee the deployment.
Your Role
You are an AI governance consultant advising the bank's CEO on what governance structures to establish before go-live.
Analysis
Role clarity first (I.B.1): Assigning oversight to the CISO alone is a structural flaw. Credit decisioning implicates Legal, Risk, Compliance, Business and Privacy. A cross-functional oversight committee must be constituted with clear RACI.

Deployer obligations (I.B.5): The bank is the deployer. Its obligations include assessing use-case fit, establishing human override procedures for adverse decisions, notifying affected customers, and managing incidents. The vendor (provider) owes the bank technical documentation and a post-market monitoring plan — negotiate these before signing.

Proportionate structure (I.B.4): A mid-size bank does not need a dedicated CAIO immediately, but it does need a named accountability owner, a documented escalation path, and a review gate before each major model update.

Training gap (I.B.3): Credit officers who will rely on or override model outputs need training — not on the model's maths, but on what the score means, when to escalate, and how to document override decisions.
Outcome
The bank establishes a Credit AI Governance Committee (Legal, Risk, Compliance, IT, Business), negotiates a data sheet and conformity statement from the vendor, runs a tabletop exercise before go-live, and delivers a 90-minute training session to all credit officers. Proportionate governance for a mid-size deployer in a regulated sector.

Recall Quick Recall — Click to Reveal ›
What two rationales does the BOK specify for cross-functional collaboration in AI governance? ▸
Efficacy and diversity of expertise and perspective (I.B.2 verbatim).
Name all six variables the BOK specifies for differentiating governance approaches (I.B.4). ▸
Company size, maturity, industry, products and services, objectives and risk tolerance.
A company integrates a third-party AI tool into its HR screening process. Which BOK role does it occupy? ▸
Deployer — it is operating the AI system in a specific context, not building or distributing it.
What three content areas must a governance training program cover per I.B.3? ▸
AI terminology, strategy and governance.
Can a single organisation be both a developer and a deployer simultaneously? ▸
Yes. A bank that builds its own fraud-detection model and uses it internally occupies both roles and carries both sets of obligations.
What is the provider's primary governance obligation that the deployer does not share? ▸
Conformity assessment, instructions for use to deployers, and post-market monitoring — these flow from making the system available to others.
Why is assigning AI governance solely to the CISO a structural flaw under I.B? ▸
AI governance spans Legal, Risk, Compliance, HR, Business and Privacy. CISO-only ownership violates I.B.1 (clear role definition) and I.B.2 (cross-functional mandate) — security is one dimension of the problem, not the whole.
