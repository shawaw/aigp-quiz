Testing & Documentation

Govern the collection and use of data in training and testing the AI model and system
BOK Performance Indicators — III.B
  1. III.B.1 Establish and follow the requirements for data governance (e.g., assess and document lawful rights to collect and use data, and assess data quality, quantity, integrity and fit-for-purpose).
  2. III.B.2 Establish and document data lineage and provenance.
  3. III.B.3 Plan and perform training and testing of the AI model and system (e.g., unit, integration, validation, performance, security, bias and interpretability).
  4. III.B.4 Identify and manage issues and risks during training and testing of the AI model and system.
  5. III.B.5 Document the training and testing process (e.g., to validate results, establish compliance and manage risks).
Concepts Key Concepts ▾
Testing Sequencing and the "Right Order" Rule (III.B.3)

The seven testing types in III.B.3 follow a logical dependency chain — each type presupposes the previous stages are complete. Sequencing failures are a high-value exam trap.

Seven BOK-enumerated testing types in sequence
Unit — validates each isolated model component. Cannot detect interface problems until individual parts work independently.
Integration — verifies assembled components interface correctly, including failure modes when connected systems fail to transmit data. Run after unit testing.
Validation — confirms the model addresses the defined business problem across its full operational scope. Must precede performance testing — you cannot meaningfully assess "how well" until you confirm "what for."
Performance — measures accuracy, throughput, latency, and reliability. Only meaningful once use-case fit is validated.
Security — probes adversarial vulnerabilities: training data extraction (memorisation attacks), model inversion, adversarial input manipulation, prompt injection in generative models. Not optional for models trained on sensitive data.
Bias — evaluates differential outcomes across protected or demographic groups at granular (not just aggregate) level.
Interpretability — assesses whether specific decisions can be explained to regulators, users, or affected individuals. High aggregate accuracy does not substitute for individual-decision explainability.
Mnemonic
"Uncle Ivan Validates Performance — Security, Bias, Interpretability"

Exam trap: When a question describes a team "skipping" a testing type and asks what the gap is, identify the type based on what the scenario is missing — not what comes next chronologically. The question is usually about scope, not sequence.

Validation vs Performance: The Most Misapplied Distinction (III.B.3)

The exam will present scenarios where a model achieves high performance metrics and ask whether testing is complete. The most common trap is conflating metric quality with use-case validation.

Testing TypeCore QuestionWhat It Does Not Answer
ValidationAre we building the right model for the right problem across the full operational scope?Does not measure accuracy, speed, fairness, or security
PerformanceHow accurately, quickly, and reliably does the model perform its function?Does not confirm use-case fit, fairness, or explainability

Validation failures often manifest as coverage gaps: a test set drawn from only one sub-population, environment, or time window cannot validate a model intended for broader use. The 96% accuracy is real — but for whom, when, and where?

Risk Taxonomy During Training and Testing (III.B.4)

III.B.4 requires identifying AND managing risks — suppressing a finding is a management failure even when the identification was correct. The four main risk categories the exam targets:

Key risk categories
Data quality risk — corrupt, mislabelled, or swapped-field training records producing unreliable outputs from the start. Governed jointly by III.B.1 (data integrity) and III.B.4.
Bias risk — systematic performance differences across demographic or protected-attribute groups. Suppressing a bias finding and proceeding to deployment is a III.B.4 violation, not merely an ethics concern.
Distribution shift / drift risk — real-world inputs after deployment differ from training distribution. Three sub-types the exam tests: (1) temporal shift (post-pandemic borrower behaviour); (2) equipment/source shift (community clinic vs academic hospital imaging); (3) policy-induced shift (hospital discharge criteria change). Governance response: temporal validation, representativeness review, retraining trigger planning.
Data leakage risk — test data contamination of training produces falsely optimistic metrics. Distinct from drift: leakage inflates test performance; drift causes post-deployment degradation without inflated test metrics.

Distinguishing drift from overfitting (exam trap): Overfitting produces poor generalisation immediately visible in validation testing. Drift produces good test performance then post-deployment degradation — the model was well-fitted to the training distribution; the world changed. The timeline (when does performance degrade?) is the diagnostic.

Documentation Adequacy: The Three-Purpose Standard (III.B.5)

III.B.5 requires documentation sufficient to fulfil three distinct purposes. The exam tests whether a described artefact meets all three — or only some.

PurposeWhat It RequiresWhat Does Not Satisfy It
Validate resultsRecords sufficient to reproduce and verify what the model achieved in testingSummary metrics without run configurations or selection criteria
Establish complianceRecords demonstrating lawful data use, testing scope, and risk assessment for regulatorsInternal slide decks, vague data-source labels ("internal company data")
Manage risksRisk-category-specific records (bias results by subgroup, security findings, drift triggers)Aggregate performance metrics without risk-category breakdown

A management summary slide deck may validate results in a business sense, but it cannot establish compliance or enable operational risk management. The three purposes require different levels of technical granularity.

Retraining Is a New Training Event (III.B.5)

Each retraining cycle is a new training and testing event — the documentation obligations under III.B.5 apply fully to each cycle, not only to the original training. If training data changes, the lineage record (III.B.2) must be updated. Stale documentation from the original cycle cannot validate, establish compliance for, or enable risk management of a model now trained on different data.

III.B vs III.C boundary (exam trap): Retraining the model → III.B (training and testing). Monitoring the deployed model without retraining → III.C (release, monitoring and maintenance). A question asking "which PI governs quarterly retraining" is asking about III.B, not III.C.2.

Scenario Worked Scenario — TechDrive Autonomous Vehicle Lane Detection ▾
Situation
TechDrive Ltd develops an AI model for autonomous vehicle lane detection. The model is tested on 10,000 hours of highway driving data (flat roads, clear lane markings, daylight) and achieves 99.7% accuracy. The team declares it ready for nationwide deployment covering urban streets, roundabouts, and construction zones.
Spot Three III.B Governance Failures

1. III.B.3 Validation failure: The test set covers only highway conditions. Validation testing must confirm the model solves the intended problem — lane detection across all operational environments — before performance metrics are accepted. 99.7% accuracy on highways does not validate suitability for urban or construction-zone deployment.

2. III.B.4 Distribution shift risk not identified: The team did not flag that urban environments, roundabouts, and construction zones represent distribution shift from the training data. This risk should have been identified and tested against before deployment was approved.

3. III.B.5 Documentation gap: A model card recording only "99.7% accuracy — highway test dataset" does not establish compliance (no record of operational scope limitations) or enable risk management (no documentation of excluded environments). A regulator or deployer relying on this card cannot assess deployment suitability for urban use.

Governance Response
Pause deployment. Expand test set to include urban, roundabout, and construction zone scenarios. Conduct validation testing against full operational scope. Update documentation to record testing scope and known limitations. Only after validation across all intended environments is the model deployable.
Recall Quick Recall — Click to Reveal ▾
What does validation testing confirm that performance testing does not? ▸
Validation confirms the model is solving the right problem across its full operational scope (use-case fit). Performance measures how well it does that task. You cannot meaningfully assess performance until use-case fit is validated.
A team conducts bias testing and finds the model disadvantages one group. They proceed to deployment without disclosing the finding. Which PI is violated? ▸
III.B.4 — Identify AND manage risks. Identification without management (disclosure, mitigation, or escalation) is a direct III.B.4 violation. Suppressing a finding is a risk management failure.
What is the difference between data drift and overfitting in terms of when performance degrades? ▸
Overfitting: poor generalisation visible immediately in validation testing. Drift: good test performance, then post-deployment degradation — the model fit its training distribution well; the world changed.
A model card says "trained on internal company data." Which III.B.5 purpose(s) does this vague description fail to satisfy? ▸
All three — it cannot validate results (no data composition to verify), cannot establish compliance (insufficient for regulators assessing lawful use), and cannot manage risks (no lineage information to diagnose future issues).
Which testing type under III.B.3 specifically addresses training data memorisation and extraction in generative AI models? ▸
Security testing — which probes adversarial vulnerabilities including model inversion and training data extraction. A chatbot that reproduces other users' personal data from training is a security testing failure (III.B.3), not an integration failure.
A model is retrained quarterly. Only the performance metric is updated in the model card. What is the III.B.5 governance issue? ▸
Each retraining is a new training event. III.B.5 documentation obligations apply fully to each cycle — training data, methodology, and testing results for each cycle must be documented. Stale original documentation cannot validate results or establish compliance for the new model version.
What is the difference between data leakage risk and distribution shift risk? ▸
Data leakage: test set contamination of training produces inflated test metrics visible before deployment. Distribution shift: real-world inputs after deployment diverge from training distribution — test metrics look fine, performance degrades post-deployment.
