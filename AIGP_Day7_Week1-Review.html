BOK Performance Indicators — Week 1 (Verbatim)

I.A — Understand what AI is and why it needs governance

  • I.A.1 Know the generally accepted definitions and types of AI.
  • I.A.2 Identify the types of risks and harms posed by AI to individuals, groups, organizations and society (e.g., misalignment with objectives, ethics and bias risk, and complexity and scalability).
  • I.A.3 Identify the unique characteristics of AI that require a comprehensive approach to governance (e.g., complexity, opacity, autonomy, speed and scale, potential for harm or misuse, data dependency, and probabilistic versus deterministic outputs).
  • I.A.4 Identify and apply the common principles of responsible AI (e.g., fairness, safety and reliability, privacy and security, transparency and explainability, accountability and human-centricity).

I.B — Establish and communicate organizational expectations for AI governance

  • I.B.1 Define roles and responsibilities for AI governance stakeholders.
  • I.B.2 Establish cross-functional collaboration in the AI governance program (e.g., for efficacy and diversity of expertise and perspective).
  • I.B.3 Create and deliver a training and awareness program to all stakeholders on AI terminology, strategy and governance.
  • I.B.4 Differentiate approaches to AI governance based upon company size, maturity, industry, products and services, objectives and risk tolerance.
  • I.B.5 Identify differences among AI developers, providers, deployers and users from a governance perspective (e.g., with respect to responsibilities, opportunities and needs).

I.C — Establish policies and procedures to apply throughout the AI life cycle

  • I.C.1 Create and implement policies to ensure oversight and accountability across all AI life cycle stages (e.g., use case assessment, risk management, ethics by design, data acquisition and use, model and system development, training and testing, deployment and monitoring, documentation and reporting, and incident management).
  • I.C.2 Evaluate and update existing policies (e.g., data privacy, security, data governance and intellectual property) for AI.
  • I.C.3 Create, update and implement policies, assessments and contracts to manage third-party risk (e.g., procurement, supply chain, human resources and acceptable use).

II.A — Understand how existing data privacy laws apply to AI

  • II.A.1 Understand how transparency, choice, lawful basis and purpose limitation requirements apply to AI.
  • II.A.2 Understand how data minimization and privacy by design requirements apply to AI.
  • II.A.3 Understand how obligations on data controllers apply to AI (e.g., regarding privacy impact assessments, use of third-party processors, cross-border data transfers, data subject rights, automated decision making, incident management, breach notification and record keeping).
  • II.A.4 Understand the requirements that apply to sensitive or special categories of data (e.g., biometrics).

II.B — Understand how other types of existing laws apply to AI

  • II.B.1 Understand how intellectual property laws apply to AI (e.g., prohibiting or limiting use of data for AI training).
  • II.B.2 Understand how nondiscrimination laws apply to AI (e.g., in the employment, credit, lending, housing and insurance contexts).
  • II.B.3 Understand how consumer protection laws apply to AI (e.g., prohibiting unfair and deceptive acts or practices).
  • II.B.4 Understand how product liability laws apply to AI (e.g., prohibiting design and manufacturing defects).

II.C — Understand the main elements of AI-specific laws

  • II.C.1 Understand the risk classification framework for AI (e.g., prohibited/high/limited/minimal risk) and what systems/uses fall into each category.
  • II.C.2 Understand the key requirements around risk management, data governance, technical documentation, conformity/impact assessments and record keeping.
  • II.C.3 Understand the key requirements around human oversight, transparency and notification, and quality management.
  • II.C.4 Understand the distinct requirements for general-purpose AI models.
  • II.C.5 Understand the enforcement framework and penalties for noncompliance.
  • II.C.6 Understand the differences in requirements based on organizational context (e.g., providers, deployers, importers and distributors).

II.D — Understand the main industry standards and tools that apply to AI

  • II.D.1 Understand the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) principles, framework, policies and recommended practices for trustworthy AI.
  • II.D.2 Understand the NIST AI Risk Management Framework and Playbook (e.g., core functions, categories and subcategories).
  • II.D.3 Understand the core ISO AI standards (i.e., 22989, 42001 and 42005).
Concepts High-Yield Synthesis Points ▶
EU AI Act Risk Tiers (II.C.1) — Your Weakest PI

The EU AI Act classifies all AI systems into four risk tiers. The exam tests whether you can place a system correctly and know the consequences of each classification.

Why it matters: II.C.1 was your lowest-scoring PI at 33% accuracy. The classification decision is the gateway to all downstream obligations — get the tier wrong and every obligation answer follows incorrectly.

Prohibited: Systems that pose unacceptable risk — social scoring by public authorities, real-time biometric remote identification in public spaces (with narrow law enforcement exceptions), subliminal manipulation, exploitation of vulnerabilities of specific groups, emotion recognition in workplace/education, AI that infers protected attributes from biometrics.
High risk: Systems listed in Annex III — biometric categorization, critical infrastructure, educational access, employment decisions, essential private/public services (e.g., credit scoring, insurance), law enforcement, migration/border control, administration of justice. Also AI in products under existing EU product safety legislation (Annex I).
Limited risk: Systems with transparency obligations only — chatbots (must disclose AI), emotion recognition systems, deepfakes. No conformity assessment; users just need to know.
Minimal risk: All other AI — spam filters, AI-powered video games. No mandatory requirements. Voluntary codes of conduct encouraged.

Exam trap: A credit-scoring AI is HIGH risk (Annex III — essential private services), not limited risk. A customer service chatbot is LIMITED risk (transparency only). A social scoring tool for government benefits allocation is PROHIBITED.

Provider vs Deployer — The Governance Split (I.B.5 + II.C.6)

The distinction between AI chain roles is one of the most tested concepts across Domains I–IV. It cuts through every governance decision.

Why it matters: Obligations differ materially based on role. The AIGP exam tests your ability to allocate responsibility correctly in scenario questions.

Developer: Builds the underlying AI model or technology. May or may not place it on the market. Carries IP, training data, and foundational design obligations.
Provider (EU AI Act): Places the AI system on the EU market or puts it into service — regardless of whether they built it. Carries primary compliance obligations: technical documentation, conformity assessment, CE marking, registration in EU database, post-market monitoring.
Deployer: Uses the AI system in their own activity (not placing it on the market). Carries secondary obligations: use in conformity with instructions, human oversight, incident reporting, fundamental rights impact assessment (for certain public sector uses), data governance for own inputs.
Importer/Distributor: Importers ensure provider compliance before placing on EU market. Distributors verify CE marking and documentation are in order.
User (general): End person interacting with the AI output. Fewer formal obligations but rights protection applies (e.g., right to explanation for ADM).

Exam trap: A bank that procures a third-party credit scoring model and uses it for lending decisions is a DEPLOYER, not a provider — even though it bears significant regulatory exposure under both the AI Act and nondiscrimination law.

GPAI Models — Distinct Requirements (II.C.4)

General-purpose AI (GPAI) models are AI models trained on large amounts of data that can perform a wide variety of tasks. They introduce a separate regulatory layer under the EU AI Act.

Why it matters: II.C.4 was at 50% accuracy. GPAI is a relatively new regulatory concept; the exam tests whether you distinguish GPAI obligations from high-risk AI obligations.

All GPAI providers must: Maintain and provide technical documentation; comply with EU copyright law (opt-out for TDM); publish a sufficiently detailed summary of training data.
GPAI with systemic risk (additional): Models trained above 10^25 FLOPs are presumed systemic risk. Extra obligations: model evaluation including adversarial testing (red-teaming); assess and mitigate systemic risks; report serious incidents to the Commission; ensure cybersecurity protection.
Key distinction: GPAI obligations attach to the model itself (by the company that trained it), not to downstream deployers who integrate it into applications. The downstream deployer may still face high-risk AI obligations for the system they build with the model.
Automated Decision-Making and Data Subject Rights (II.A.3)

Under GDPR-aligned frameworks, data controllers using AI for automated decision-making face specific obligations. This is the intersection of privacy law and AI governance that the exam tests heavily.

Why it matters: II.A.3 covers the broadest set of data controller obligations. ADM is the sub-obligation most commonly tested in scenario questions involving AI-driven decisions (hiring, credit, insurance).

Art. 22 GDPR (ADM right): Data subjects have the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing — including profiling — that produces legal or similarly significant effects. Exceptions: necessary for contract; authorised by law; explicit consent. Requires human review option.
Privacy Impact Assessment (DPIA): Required for high-risk processing — AI systems using biometrics, large-scale profiling, systematic monitoring of public spaces. Must assess necessity, proportionality, and risk mitigation.
Cross-border transfers: AI systems often process data across jurisdictions. Adequacy decisions, SCCs, BCRs, or derogations required. AI training on data from multiple countries implicates each country's transfer rules.
Standards Landscape: NIST, OECD, ISO (II.D)

Three frameworks shape voluntary (and increasingly mandatory) AI governance practice. The exam tests their structure and purpose, not deep technical detail.

Why it matters: II.D.2 (NIST) was at 50% accuracy. The exam distinguishes the frameworks by their function, not just their names.

NIST AI RMF (II.D.2): Four core functions — GOVERN (establish structure, policies, culture), MAP (identify context and risks), MEASURE (analyze and assess risks), MANAGE (prioritize and respond). The Playbook provides specific suggested actions under each function's categories and subcategories. Voluntary but widely adopted; referenced by US federal agencies.
OECD AI Principles (II.D.1): Five principles for trustworthy AI — inclusive growth and well-being; human-centred values and fairness; transparency and explainability; robustness, security and safety; accountability. Plus five recommendations for policymakers (investment in R&D, enabling environment, governance frameworks, building capabilities, international cooperation).
ISO AI Standards (II.D.3): ISO 22989 (AI concepts and terminology — definitions standard); ISO 42001 (AI management system — AIMS, the "ISO 9001 for AI"); ISO 42005 (AI system impact assessment — methodology for assessing AI impacts, complementary to 42001).

Exam trap: ISO 42001 is the management system standard (organizational requirements). ISO 42005 is the impact assessment standard. ISO 22989 is terminology only. NIST is a risk management framework (voluntary, US origin). OECD sets principles (intergovernmental, non-binding).

Lifecycle Policies — The Full Coverage Requirement (I.C.1)

I.C.1 requires policies across every stage of the AI lifecycle. Exam scenarios test whether you identify the correct policy stage for a given governance gap.

Why it matters: The lifecycle framing is the backbone of Domains III and IV. Exam questions in Week 1 use it to test policy design; Week 2 tests implementation of the same stages.

Nine lifecycle stages in I.C.1: Use case assessment · Risk management · Ethics by design · Data acquisition and use · Model and system development · Training and testing · Deployment and monitoring · Documentation and reporting · Incident management.
I.C.2 — Updating existing policies: Data privacy, security, data governance, and IP policies must be updated (not just supplemented) to address AI-specific requirements. A general data governance policy without AI-specific provisions is insufficient.
I.C.3 — Third-party risk: Procurement contracts, supply chain agreements, HR policies, and acceptable use policies all require AI-specific clauses. Standard vendor risk management processes need to be extended to cover AI-specific risks (model drift, data rights, liability allocation).

Scenario Cross-Cutting: Bank Deploys Third-Party Credit AI ▶
Situation FinBank SG procures an automated credit-scoring AI system from CreditTech, a Singapore-registered vendor that trained the model on consumer credit data from multiple jurisdictions. FinBank integrates it into its personal loan application process. The system ranks applicants and rejects those below a threshold score without human review. A disproportionate rejection rate is observed for applicants from certain ethnic communities.
Role You are FinBank's AI Governance Lead. A regulator requests your governance response.
Analysis Risk classification (II.C.1): Credit scoring for individuals is a High-risk use case under EU AI Act Annex III. As the party placing this into service in their operations, FinBank is the deployer — but if FinBank has configured the system substantially, it may take on provider obligations (II.C.6).

Third-party governance (I.C.3): FinBank's procurement contract with CreditTech should have required technical documentation, model cards, bias testing results, and contractual rights to audit. Absence of these provisions is a governance failure under I.C.3.

Nondiscrimination (II.B.2): Differential rejection rates across ethnic groups may constitute unlawful discrimination in credit/lending contexts. FinBank must investigate whether the AI's outputs correlate with protected characteristics and constitute disparate impact.

ADM obligations (II.A.3): Rejecting loan applications without human review constitutes solely automated decision-making with significant legal effect. GDPR-aligned frameworks require FinBank to provide applicants the right to obtain human review, express their point of view, and contest the decision.

Data minimization (II.A.2): Training data sourced from multiple jurisdictions requires lawful basis for each. Cross-border transfer rules apply where the model was trained on data transferred from GDPR-regulated jurisdictions.
Outcome FinBank must (1) suspend fully automated rejections pending human review process implementation; (2) commission a bias audit of the model; (3) update procurement contracts to require ongoing bias monitoring and right to audit; (4) assess whether AI Act deployer obligations (fundamental rights impact assessment) apply; (5) review DPIA under applicable privacy law. This scenario crosses I.C.3, II.A.2, II.A.3, II.B.2, II.C.1, and II.C.6.

Recall Quick Recall Flashcards ▶
What are the four EU AI Act risk tiers, and which one applies to credit scoring?▾
Prohibited, High, Limited, Minimal. Credit scoring = High risk (Annex III, essential private services). [II.C.1]
What is the difference between a provider and a deployer under the EU AI Act?▾
Provider: places the AI system on the market or puts it into service — carries primary technical documentation, conformity assessment, and registration obligations. Deployer: uses an AI system in their own operations — carries secondary obligations including human oversight, incident reporting, and using the system as instructed. [I.B.5, II.C.6]
What are the four NIST AI RMF core functions?▾
GOVERN · MAP · MEASURE · MANAGE. Govern sets structure and culture; Map identifies risks in context; Measure analyzes and prioritizes; Manage implements responses and monitors. [II.D.2]
What three ISO standards cover AI, and what does each address?▾
ISO 22989: AI concepts and terminology (definitions). ISO 42001: AI management system — organizational requirements (the AIMS standard). ISO 42005: AI system impact assessment methodology. [II.D.3]
Under GDPR-aligned law, when does the right not to be subject to automated decision-making apply?▾
When a decision is based solely on automated processing (including profiling) and produces legal or similarly significant effects. Exceptions: contract necessity, legal authorization, or explicit consent — but in each case the controller must still offer human review on request. [II.A.3]
What additional obligations apply to GPAI models with systemic risk?▾
On top of baseline GPAI obligations: adversarial testing (red-teaming) before release; systemic risk assessment and mitigation; incident reporting to the Commission; cybersecurity protection. Systemic risk is presumed above 10^25 FLOPs training compute. [II.C.4]
What nine lifecycle stages must be covered by I.C.1 policies?▾
Use case assessment · Risk management · Ethics by design · Data acquisition and use · Model and system development · Training and testing · Deployment and monitoring · Documentation and reporting · Incident management. [I.C.1]
