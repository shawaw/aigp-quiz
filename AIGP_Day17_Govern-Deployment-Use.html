IV.C — Govern the Deployment and Use of the AI System
BOK Performance Indicators ▾
  1. IV.C.1 Apply the policies, procedures, best practices and ethical considerations to the deployment of an AI system (e.g., data governance, risk management, issue management, and user training).
  2. IV.C.2 Conduct continuous monitoring of the AI model and system, and establish a regular schedule for maintenance, updates and retraining.
  3. IV.C.3 Conduct periodic activities to assess the AI system's performance, reliability and safety (e.g., audits, red teaming, threat modeling and security testing).
  4. IV.C.4 Document incidents, issues, risks and post-market monitoring plans.
  5. IV.C.5 Forecast and reduce risks of secondary or unintended uses and downstream harms.
  6. IV.C.6 Establish external communication plans.
  7. IV.C.7 Create and implement a policy and controls to deactivate or localize an AI system as necessary (e.g., due to regulatory requirements or performance issues).
Concepts Key Concepts ▾
IV.C.1 — Deployment Governance

IV.C.1 is the deployer's obligation to apply the full governance stack at the point of deployment and use. The BOK's examples are a checklist, not a suggestion: data governance, risk management, issue management, and user training. Each can fail independently at deployment even when it was handled correctly during development.

Exam use: questions will describe a deployment gap — missing user training, no issue escalation path, uncontrolled data access — and ask which PI is implicated. IV.C.1 is the broadest deployer obligation and is the correct answer when no more specific PI fits the scenario.
Key distinctions
IV.C.1 vs III.A.3 III.A.3 applies policies during design and build. IV.C.1 applies them at deployment and ongoing use. Same governance frame, different life cycle stage.
User training Explicitly listed under IV.C.1. Staff deploying the system without understanding its limitations is a IV.C.1 violation — not a training program issue under I.B.3 Organizational Governance.
IV.C.2 — Continuous Monitoring & Retraining

IV.C.2 requires ongoing, operational visibility into the AI system's behaviour and a scheduled cadence for maintenance, updates and retraining. Data drift, concept drift and distributional shift are the failure modes this PI is designed to catch before they cause harm.

Critical distinction: IV.C.2 and IV.C.3 are frequently confused on the exam. IV.C.2 = continuous, real-time, operational (dashboards, automated alerts, retraining schedules triggered by drift). IV.C.3 = periodic, structured, assessment-oriented (audits, red teaming, threat modeling). A real-time alert dashboard is IV.C.2. A quarterly security audit is IV.C.3.
Parallel with III.C
III.C.2 vs IV.C.2 Near-identical obligations. Both developer and deployer must conduct continuous monitoring. The responsible party differs; the activity does not.
IV.C.3 — Periodic Assessment

IV.C.3 requires scheduled, structured assessments going beyond routine monitoring. The BOK examples — audits, red teaming, threat modeling, security testing — signal deliberately intrusive activities designed to find what continuous monitoring misses, including adversarial vulnerabilities and systemic reliability gaps.

Red teaming belongs under IV.C.3, not IV.C.2. If a question mentions adversarial probing, simulated attacks, or structured vulnerability exercises, IV.C.3 is the answer — not IV.C.2 (continuous monitoring) and not IV.C.4 (documentation).
IV.C.4 — Incident Documentation & Post-Market Monitoring Plans

IV.C.4 has two distinct components: (1) ongoing formal documentation of incidents, issues and risks as they occur; and (2) a post-market monitoring plan — a structured forward-looking document outlining how the deployer will track the system's performance after release. The plan is a deliverable in its own right, not a summary of past incident logs.

The exam will test whether you notice that III.C.4 says "manage and document incidents, issues and risks" while IV.C.4 explicitly adds "post-market monitoring plans." Any question asking which PI requires a post-market monitoring plan points to IV.C.4, not III.C.4.
What must be documented
Incidents Discrete events — system errors, unexpected outputs, harm events — regardless of perceived severity
Issues Ongoing performance problems, persistent gaps between expected and actual behaviour
Risks Identified potential harms not yet realised, retained in the risk register
Post-market monitoring plan Proactive, structured plan for ongoing oversight — distinct from reactive incident logs
IV.C.5 — Secondary Uses & Downstream Harms

IV.C.5 is deployer-specific with no direct parallel in III.C. It requires the governance team to actively forecast how the AI system might be used beyond its validated scope, and to reduce the harms those unintended uses could cause. "Secondary use" means use cases the system was not designed or validated for. "Downstream harms" refers to effects on individuals, groups or society flowing from the system's outputs — even indirectly.

IV.C.5 is the answer when a scenario shows harms arising indirectly: proxy discrimination, scope creep by end users, or outputs used in decisions the model was not validated for. Don't confuse with IV.C.3 (periodic audits) or IV.C.2 (monitoring) even when the harm was detectable through those activities — IV.C.5 is the obligation to proactively forecast and prevent.
Examples
Secondary use A clinical note summarization model repurposed by a clinician to generate patient prognosis estimates it was not validated for
Downstream harm A home insurance pricing model producing racially correlated outcomes through postal code proxy variables
IV.C.6 — External Communication Plans

IV.C.6 requires the deployer to establish plans for communicating with external stakeholders — regulators, affected users, the public — about the AI system. This is operational communication about deployment and incidents, not technical documentation for the supply chain.

III.C.6 (developer) covers public disclosures — technical documentation, instructions for use to deployers, post-market monitoring plans sent through the supply chain. IV.C.6 (deployer) covers external communication plans aimed at regulators, users and the public. A question about a regulatory enquiry response or a user notification plan points to IV.C.6.
IV.C.7 — Deactivation & Localization Controls

IV.C.7 requires a policy and operational controls to take the AI system fully offline or restrict it to a subset of markets or use cases when necessary. The BOK gives two explicit triggers: regulatory requirements and performance issues. Both are mandatory — if either condition arises, the deployer must be capable of acting immediately.

"Localization" is the key term candidates miss. It means restricting the system geographically or by use scope rather than full shutdown. A question describing a regional compliance failure or market-specific performance problem where the deployer restricts (not suspends) the system maps to IV.C.7 "localize." Both deactivation and localization require a pre-existing policy — not an ad hoc decision.
Both triggers are explicit in the BOK
Regulatory requirements Legal basis suspended, re-certification mandated, jurisdiction-specific prohibition enacted
Performance issues Reliability failure, safety breach, unresolvable model drift, regional language model gap

Scenario Regional Insurer — Three Simultaneous Governance Failures ▾
Situation You are the AI Governance Lead at a regional insurer that deployed a third-party AI underwriting model 12 months ago. This week: (a) a regulator requests your post-market monitoring plan; (b) a broker reports the model is producing inconsistent premium quotes for identical risk profiles; and (c) your IT team reveals three model errors were logged last month but never escalated or recorded formally.
Analysis The regulator request: IV.C.4 requires a formal post-market monitoring plan as a documented deliverable. An incident log is not a substitute. The inconsistent quotes: IV.C.2 requires continuous monitoring to detect output drift — identical inputs producing different outputs is a drift signal your monitoring should have caught before external escalation. The three undocumented errors: IV.C.4 again — all incidents must be formally documented regardless of perceived severity or resolution outcome. The regulator interaction also activates IV.C.6 — your external communication plan governs how your organisation responds to regulatory queries about AI system performance.
Outcome Immediate: create the post-market monitoring plan and backfill the incident register for the three undocumented errors (IV.C.4). Parallel: investigate the inconsistent quotes through your monitoring infrastructure and schedule a model review (IV.C.2). Draft the regulator response under your external communication plan (IV.C.6). If the investigation confirms the inconsistency cannot be resolved without model replacement, invoke IV.C.7 controls to localize or suspend the system in affected product lines until a validated model is in place.
Recall Quick Recall ▾
What four examples does IV.C.1 give for deployer governance? ▸
Data governance, risk management, issue management, and user training. All four are explicitly listed in the BOK under IV.C.1.
IV.C.2 vs IV.C.3 — what is the operational difference? ▸
IV.C.2 is continuous and operational: real-time monitoring, automated drift alerts, scheduled retraining. IV.C.3 is periodic and structured: audits, red teaming, threat modeling, security testing.
What does IV.C.4 require that III.C.4 does not? ▸
Post-market monitoring plans. III.C.4 says "manage and document incidents, issues and risks." IV.C.4 explicitly adds "post-market monitoring plans" as a required deliverable.
The two explicit BOK triggers for IV.C.7 deactivation or localization? ▸
Regulatory requirements (e.g., suspended legal basis, re-certification mandated) and performance issues (e.g., reliability failure, unresolvable drift). Both are explicit in the PI text.
Which IV.C PIs have no parallel in III.C? ▸
IV.C.5 (forecast and reduce secondary use risks and downstream harms) and IV.C.7 (deactivation and localization controls). Both are deployer-specific obligations with no developer equivalent.
IV.C.5 vs IV.C.3: a model produces racially biased outcomes through proxy variables. Which PI? ▸
IV.C.5 — proxy-variable bias is an unintended downstream harm. IV.C.3 audits might detect it, but IV.C.5 is the obligation to forecast and proactively prevent such harms from arising.
Why does IV.C carry the most exam weight of any single competency? ▸
9–11 questions, the highest weight for any single competency in the AIGP exam. It has 7 PIs spanning the full deployer governance lifecycle — missing any one PI costs exam marks directly.
