III.C — Govern the Release, Monitoring and Maintenance of the AI System

BOKPerformance Indicators
  • III.C.1 Assess readiness, and prepare for release into production (e.g., creating the model card and satisfying conformity requirements).
  • III.C.2 Conduct continuous monitoring of the AI system, and establish a regular schedule for maintenance, updates and retraining.
  • III.C.3 Conduct periodic activities to assess the AI system's performance, reliability and safety (e.g., audits, red teaming, threat modeling and security testing).
  • III.C.4 Manage and document incidents, issues and risks.
  • III.C.5 Collaborate with cross-functional stakeholders to understand why incidents arise from AI systems (e.g., brittleness, lack of robustness, lack of quality data, insufficient testing, and model or data drift).
  • III.C.6 Make public disclosures to meet transparency obligations (e.g., technical documentation, instructions for use to deployers and post-market monitoring plans).
Concept 1Release Readiness & Model Cards (III.C.1)▸
What release readiness requires

Before an AI system enters production, the governance professional must confirm three pillars are satisfied: technical readiness (performance thresholds met, bias checks cleared), documentation readiness (model card complete), and conformity readiness (regulatory or standards requirements fulfilled — e.g., EU AI Act conformity assessment for high-risk systems). Release is a gate, not a handoff.

Why it matters on the exam: III.C.1 names two specific examples — the model card and conformity requirements. Questions will present release packages missing one of these and ask what the governance professional should do. The correct answer is always to block release until both are present.

Key terms
Model card A structured, standardised disclosure document accompanying an AI model that records its intended use, performance metrics across demographic groups, known limitations, and evaluation conditions. Created at release, not after.
Conformity assessment A formal verification process used under the EU AI Act and similar frameworks that confirms a high-risk AI system meets applicable legal and technical requirements before market placement.
Release gate An organisational control point where defined criteria must be met before deployment proceeds. The governance professional enforces this gate — it is not advisory.
Concept 2Continuous Monitoring, Maintenance & Retraining (III.C.2)▸
What continuous monitoring covers — and what it drives

Continuous monitoring is always-on operational tracking of the deployed system: output quality, performance metrics, input data distribution, and anomalous patterns. Its purpose is not merely to watch — it drives the maintenance schedule. When thresholds are breached or drift is detected, monitoring triggers retraining or updates. The schedule for maintenance, updates, and retraining must be established upfront, not improvised after degradation is observed.

Why it matters on the exam: The exam distinguishes continuous monitoring (III.C.2) from periodic assessment (III.C.3). Continuous monitoring is automated and ongoing; periodic assessment is scheduled and episodic. A scenario involving performance degradation detected by a dashboard alert is always III.C.2. An audit or red team exercise is always III.C.3.

Key terms
Data drift A shift in the statistical properties of input data, diverging from the training distribution. Detectable through continuous monitoring; triggers retraining.
Model drift Degradation in model performance over time as the relationship between inputs and outputs changes. The primary trigger for scheduled retraining under III.C.2.
Retraining Updating the AI model using new or refreshed data to restore performance. Must be governed by a defined schedule and triggered by monitoring thresholds — not ad hoc.
Concept 3Periodic Assessment: Audits, Red Teaming & Threat Modeling (III.C.3)▸
Periodic vs. continuous — the critical distinction

Periodic assessment under III.C.3 is episodic, deeper evaluation — scheduled and deliberately resourced. The BOK lists four examples: audits (systemic compliance and performance review), red teaming (adversarial probing for vulnerabilities and biases), threat modeling (systematic risk mapping producing a threat register), and security testing (penetration tests and infrastructure hardening). None of these can run continuously because they require specialist setup, deliberate access, and often disrupt normal operations.

Why it matters on the exam: If a question asks what to do when an organisation wants to find adversarial vulnerabilities or unknown edge cases — that is III.C.3 (red teaming). If it asks what to do when a performance dashboard shows degradation — that is III.C.2. The exam will mix these scenarios and test whether you map them to the right PI.

Key terms
Red teaming A structured adversarial exercise where a specialist team probes the AI system from an attacker's perspective to find exploitable flaws, biases, or edge-case failures not visible in normal monitoring.
Threat modeling A systematic process to identify, classify, and prioritise threats to an AI system's security and reliability, producing a threat register that informs security updates and risk decisions.
AI audit A formal, often independent review of an AI system's documentation, processes, and outputs against governance requirements and performance standards. Scheduled at defined intervals.
Concept 4Incident Management & Cross-Functional Root Cause (III.C.4 & III.C.5)▸
Manage + document + diagnose — three distinct obligations

III.C.4 has two equal components: manage incidents (contain harm, escalate, remediate, verify fix) AND document them (maintain a formal log capturing what, when, who, cause, and resolution). Closing a ticket is not the same as documenting an incident. The governance professional owns the log, not the engineering team.

III.C.5 adds the diagnostic layer: AI incidents typically have cross-functional root causes. The BOK names five: brittleness (catastrophic failure on slightly different inputs), lack of robustness (failure outside training distribution), lack of quality data (training data deficiencies), insufficient testing (gaps in the test suite), and model or data drift (performance degradation over time). The governance lead must convene data scientists, risk, legal, and operations to diagnose accurately — not accept the first technical explanation offered.

Why it matters on the exam: Questions will test whether you distinguish the management obligation (III.C.4) from the diagnostic collaboration obligation (III.C.5), and whether you can classify a failure correctly among the five root causes. Brittleness = immediate catastrophic failure on slightly different input. Drift = gradual degradation over time. Insufficient testing = process gap. These are not interchangeable.

Root cause vocabulary (III.C.5)
Brittleness A model's tendency to fail catastrophically on inputs that differ only slightly from training data — a small perturbation produces a large, disproportionate error.
Lack of robustness The system's inability to maintain acceptable performance across the full range of real-world conditions, including adversarial inputs or distributional shifts.
Model or data drift Gradual degradation over time as the relationship between inputs and outputs changes (model drift) or as input data distribution shifts away from training (data drift).
Concept 5Transparency Obligations & Public Disclosures (III.C.6)▸
Three external disclosures — audience determines which applies

III.C.6 covers external disclosures only — directed at regulators, deployers, or the public, not internal stakeholders. The BOK names three examples: technical documentation (filed with regulators — covers architecture, training data, risk management, accuracy, human oversight), instructions for use to deployers (enabling downstream responsible deployment — a provider obligation), and post-market monitoring plans (forward-looking commitment to ongoing surveillance and defined review cycles).

On the exam, the audience is the key discriminator. A question about what a provider must give its enterprise customers points to instructions for use. A question about what is filed with a regulator points to technical documentation. A question about demonstrating ongoing accountability after deployment points to the post-market monitoring plan.

Why it matters: Many AI laws make failure to disclose a standalone violation — separate from any underlying system defect. Governance professionals must know what goes to whom, when, and in what form.

Key terms
Instructions for use Documentation from AI provider to deployer specifying how to use the system safely and within its intended scope. A legal requirement under the EU AI Act for high-risk systems. Directed at deployers, not the public.
Post-market monitoring plan A forward-looking disclosure committing the organisation to ongoing surveillance of the deployed system, with defined review cycles, performance thresholds, and escalation triggers.
Technical documentation Regulatory disclosure covering system architecture, training data, risk management, accuracy metrics, and human oversight mechanisms. Filed to demonstrate legal compliance.

ScenarioCredit Scoring AI: Post-Launch Governance Failure▸
Situation
A regional bank deploys a credit scoring AI in Q1. By Q3, approval rates for one demographic segment have dropped 15%. No monitoring alerts have fired. Customer complaints are being logged informally by branch managers but not centrally tracked. The bank's regulator has requested a post-market monitoring report.
Analysis
Three governance failures are visible. The approval rate shift should have triggered an alert — its absence is a failure of III.C.2. Complaints logged informally without central tracking means incidents are not being managed or documented as required by III.C.4. The 15% demographic drop requires cross-functional diagnosis from data science, risk, and legal under III.C.5. The regulator's request for a post-market monitoring report points to an additional gap under III.C.6 — the bank should have had a plan in place from launch.
Outcome
Immediate: establish a central incident register and formalise the complaint log. Convene a cross-functional root cause review. Near-term: deploy a monitoring dashboard with demographic parity alerts and defined thresholds. Produce the post-market monitoring plan for the regulator. III.C failures are most often process and organisational failures — not just technical ones.
Quick RecallFlashcards▸
What two artefacts does III.C.1 specifically name as release readiness requirements? ▸
Model card (intended use, performance across groups, limitations, evaluation conditions) and satisfying conformity requirements (regulatory/standards compliance verification).
What is the key distinction between III.C.2 and III.C.3? ▸
III.C.2 is continuous monitoring — always-on, automated, threshold-driven. III.C.3 is periodic assessment — scheduled, episodic, specialist-led (audits, red teaming, threat modeling, security testing).
Name the five root causes of AI system incidents listed in III.C.5. ▸
Brittleness, lack of robustness, lack of quality data, insufficient testing, and model or data drift.
Name the three transparency disclosures in III.C.6 and their primary audiences. ▸
Technical documentation (regulators), instructions for use (deployers), and post-market monitoring plans (regulators and public).
What does III.C.4 require beyond just fixing an incident? ▸
Documentation — a maintained log capturing what occurred, when, who was involved, what caused it, and what was done to resolve it. Management (fixing) alone is insufficient.
Brittleness vs. model drift — what is the precise distinction? ▸
Brittleness is immediate catastrophic failure on inputs slightly different from training data. Model drift is gradual performance degradation over time as the world changes.
When does continuous monitoring under III.C.2 trigger retraining? ▸
When defined performance thresholds are breached — typically from model drift or data drift detected by the monitoring system. Must follow a pre-defined schedule, not be ad hoc.
