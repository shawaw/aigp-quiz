Data Governance & Training

Govern the collection and use of data in training and testing the AI model and system
BOK Performance Indicators — III.B
  1. III.B.1 Establish and follow the requirements for data governance (e.g., assess and document lawful rights to collect and use data, and assess data quality, quantity, integrity and fit-for-purpose).
  2. III.B.2 Establish and document data lineage and provenance.
  3. III.B.3 Plan and perform training and testing of the AI model and system (e.g., unit, integration, validation, performance, security, bias and interpretability).
  4. III.B.4 Identify and manage issues and risks during training and testing of the AI model and system.
  5. III.B.5 Document the training and testing process (e.g., to validate results, establish compliance and manage risks).
Concepts Key Concepts ▾
Data Governance Requirements (III.B.1)

Before any data enters a training pipeline, the AI governance team must establish and follow requirements covering two distinct dimensions: legal rights and data quality.

Exam trap: Questions test whether you can distinguish a data quality problem from a legal rights problem — they require different governance responses. High-quality data with no lawful basis cannot be used; lawfully acquired data that is low-quality should not be used.

Key terms
Lawful rights to collect — having a legal basis (licence, consent, legitimate interest, etc.) to acquire and use data for the specific AI purpose. Purpose-specific: rights for one use do not transfer to another.
Data quality — accuracy, completeness, and consistency of the dataset.
Fit-for-purpose — the dataset is appropriate for the intended model task. A high-quality dataset for one use case may be unfit for another.
Data quantity — sufficient volume to train a statistically valid model without overfitting or underfitting.
Integrity — data has not been corrupted, altered, or tampered with during storage or transfer.
Data Lineage & Provenance (III.B.2)

Data lineage tracks how data moved through the pipeline — where it was collected, what transformations were applied, and who had access. Provenance answers where did this data originate and who owned it.

Both are prerequisites for audit and compliance. If a model is later challenged for bias or copyright infringement, lineage records are the forensic trail. No lineage = no root cause analysis.

Key terms
Lineage — end-to-end documentation of data movement, transformations, and processing steps through the pipeline.
Provenance — the origin and ownership history of a dataset, including any licences or rights attached at source.
Chain of custody — who handled the data at each stage; critical for IP claims and privacy law compliance.
Training & Testing Types (III.B.3)

The BOK enumerates seven distinct testing types. Each targets a different failure mode. Governance requires all relevant types to be planned and performed — not just performance testing.

High-value exam questions describe a scenario where one test type is missing and ask which governance gap this represents. Know precisely what each type catches.

Seven BOK-enumerated testing types
Unit — validates individual model components or functions in isolation.
Integration — verifies components work correctly when assembled into the full system.
Validation — confirms the model solves the intended business problem (are we building the right thing?).
Performance — measures accuracy, precision, recall, latency, and throughput under load.
Security — probes for adversarial vulnerabilities, data poisoning, model inversion, and prompt injection.
Bias — detects differential performance across demographic or protected groups.
Interpretability — assesses whether model decisions can be explained to regulators, users, or auditors.
Risks During Training & Testing (III.B.4)

Risk management during the training and testing phase is distinct from deployment risk. Issues identified here are cheaper to fix than post-deployment — and they propagate into the deployed model if missed.

Exam questions test whether you can classify which risk category a training-phase incident falls into and identify the correct governance response. Category matters because different risks require different mitigations.

Key risk categories
Data quality risk — corrupt, incomplete, or mislabelled training data producing unreliable model outputs.
Bias risk — underrepresentation of subgroups in training data causing differential model performance across groups.
Data leakage — test set information bleeding into training, producing falsely optimistic performance metrics.
Overfitting — model memorises training data and fails to generalise, discovered during validation testing.
Model/data drift — real-world data distribution shifts away from the training distribution post-deployment, causing degradation.
Poisoning attacks — adversarial manipulation of training data to embed backdoors or introduce targeted bias.
Documenting Training & Testing (III.B.5)

Documentation at this stage serves three explicit BOK purposes: validate results, establish compliance, and manage risks. Under the EU AI Act, technical documentation covering training data, testing methodology, and performance metrics is a compliance requirement independent of model performance.

A model can pass all tests and still be non-compliant if documentation is absent or insufficient. The documentation obligation applies equally to automated ML pipelines — there is no automation exemption.

Key documentation artefacts
Model card — standardised document covering model purpose, training data, evaluation results, limitations, and intended use.
Datasheet for datasets — documents dataset composition, collection process, labelling methodology, and known limitations.
Testing log — records each test run: hyperparameters, metrics achieved, pass/fail criteria, and tester identity.
Bias audit report — formal record of fairness evaluation methods, subgroup performance breakdown, and any remediation steps taken.

Scenario Worked Scenario ▾
Situation
A financial services firm is building a credit-scoring model using three years of historical loan data sourced from an acquired subsidiary. The data science team has completed performance testing (AUC-ROC: 0.87) and is ready to deploy. The AI governance officer flags two issues: (1) the subsidiary's data was collected under a privacy policy that predated the acquisition and did not contemplate AI scoring use; (2) no bias testing has been performed across protected demographic groups.
Role
You are the AI governance officer advising the model development team on what must be resolved before deployment can proceed.
Analysis
Issue 1 is a data governance failure under III.B.1 — the firm has not established lawful rights to use this data for AI credit scoring. The original consent did not cover this purpose, creating both a privacy law violation (purpose limitation under II.A.1) and potential contractual risk. Data lineage documentation under III.B.2 would have surfaced this earlier by recording the provenance and licensing terms of the acquired dataset. Issue 2 is a testing gap under III.B.3 — bias testing is explicitly required. Under nondiscrimination law (II.B.2), a credit-scoring model with disparate impact carries significant regulatory liability. Risk management under III.B.4 requires identifying this as a pre-deployment risk, not a post-deployment discovery.
Outcome
Deployment is halted. The team must: (1) obtain a legal opinion on whether the acquired data can be used for this purpose, and re-consent or exclude data where rights are unclear (III.B.1); (2) document data provenance as part of the lineage record (III.B.2); (3) conduct bias testing across protected attributes and document all findings (III.B.3, III.B.5). The corrected documentation becomes part of the compliance record required by technical documentation obligations under II.C.2.
Recall Quick Recall — Click to Reveal ▾
What two dimensions must III.B.1 data governance requirements address? ▸
Lawful rights to collect and use data AND data quality, quantity, integrity, and fit-for-purpose assessment.
What is the difference between data lineage and data provenance? ▸
Provenance = where data originated and its ownership history. Lineage = how data moved and was transformed throughout the pipeline. Provenance is about origin; lineage is about journey.
Name all seven testing types listed in III.B.3 in order. ▸
Unit, integration, validation, performance, security, bias, interpretability. Mnemonic: "Uncle Ivan Validates Performance — Security, Bias, Interpretability."
What is data leakage and why is it a III.B.4 training-phase risk? ▸
Test set information bleeds into training or feature engineering, producing falsely optimistic performance metrics. A flawed model appears to meet thresholds — the governance failure is undetected until deployment.
What three purposes does III.B.5 documentation explicitly serve? ▸
Validate results, establish compliance, and manage risks.
Which testing type under III.B.3 addresses whether model decisions can be explained to a regulator? ▸
Interpretability testing.
Does III.B.5 documentation apply to automated ML pipelines that self-select models? ▸
Yes. There is no automation exemption. The obligation to document the training and testing process applies regardless of whether the process is human-led or automated.
