III.A — Govern the Designing and Building of the AI System
Focus: Risk Identification & Documentation  |  Exam weight: 6–8 questions  |  Domain III (highest weight)
BOK III.A Performance Indicators (verbatim)
  1. III.A.1 Define the business context and use case of the AI system.
  2. III.A.2 Perform or review an impact assessment on the AI system.
  3. III.A.3 Apply the policies, procedures, best practices and ethical considerations to designing and building the AI system (e.g., purpose of AI, requirements gathering, architecture and model selection, human oversight, data analysis, metric and threshold evaluation, stakeholder engagement and feedback, and operational controls).
  4. III.A.4 Identify and manage the internal and external risks and contributing factors related to designing and building the AI model and system (e.g., using probability/severity harms matrix, using a risk mitigation hierarchy, stakeholder mapping, use-case evaluation, benchmarking, and pre-deployment pilots and testing).
  5. III.A.5 Document the designing and building process (e.g., to establish compliance and manage risks).
Concepts Risk Identification Tools and Documentation ▸
Probability/Severity Harms Matrix (III.A.4)

The BOK names the probability/severity harms matrix as a primary tool for identifying and managing risks during AI design. It requires rating each identified harm on two axes: likelihood of occurrence and magnitude of impact. The cross-product drives prioritisation — high-probability, high-severity harms receive the most mitigation investment.

Why it matters on the exam: questions present a scenario with multiple potential harms and ask which approach is most aligned with III.A.4. The answer always involves structured, rated evaluation of harms — not a qualitative narrative or a simple risk register.
Key distinctions
Internal risks — data quality gaps, model bias, team skill gaps, misaligned success metrics
External risks — regulatory non-compliance, third-party model behaviour, supply-chain data provenance, adversarial misuse
Contributing factors — the underlying conditions (e.g., insufficient training data diversity) that amplify a risk's probability or severity
Risk Mitigation Hierarchy (III.A.4)

A risk mitigation hierarchy ranks intervention options by effectiveness. In AI governance the BOK-implied hierarchy runs from elimination (remove the risky use case) through redesign (change architecture to remove the failure mode), then controls (technical or procedural safeguards), then monitoring (detect if a risk materialises), down to disclosure and residual risk acceptance at the bottom. Higher tiers are preferred over lower ones.

Exam trap: a question may offer "implement monitoring" as a distractor when elimination or redesign is available. The hierarchy says you apply controls in order — do not default to the easier lower-tier option when a more effective one is feasible.
Hierarchy levels (highest to lowest effectiveness)
1. Eliminate — Remove the feature, use case, or data that creates the risk
2. Redesign — Change the model architecture or system design to make the risk structurally impossible
3. Engineering controls — Technical safeguards (output filters, confidence thresholds, human-in-the-loop gates)
4. Administrative controls — Policies, training, access restrictions, acceptable-use agreements
5. Monitor and disclose — Ongoing detection plus transparency obligations; residual risk accepted
Stakeholder Mapping and Use-Case Evaluation (III.A.4)

Stakeholder mapping identifies all parties affected by or involved in the AI system — developers, deployers, end-users, regulators, and impacted third parties. It reveals who bears each risk and who must be engaged to validate the use-case scope. Use-case evaluation then stress-tests the intended application against realistic edge cases and misuse scenarios to surface additional risks before design is locked.

These two tools are often combined in scenario questions: a governance officer discovers that the original use-case scope doesn't account for a population segment — the correct action is use-case re-evaluation (III.A.4), not simply updating documentation (III.A.5).
Benchmarking and Pre-Deployment Pilots (III.A.4)

Benchmarking compares model performance against established baselines, peer models, or regulatory standards before finalising the design. Pre-deployment pilots and testing (unit, integration, performance, bias, security) run the system in a controlled environment before production release. Both are explicitly listed as III.A.4 tools — they reduce the probability of harms materialising post-launch.

Distinguish from III.C.3 (periodic audits, red teaming post-deployment). Benchmarking and pilots happen during design and build — if a question says "before release" or "during development", the answer is III.A.4, not III.C.
Documentation of the Design and Build Process (III.A.5)

III.A.5 requires documenting the designing and building process with two explicit purposes: (1) to establish compliance — demonstrating that policies, ethical considerations, and regulatory requirements were applied; and (2) to manage risks — creating an evidence trail that enables risk review, audit, and remediation. Documentation is not a post-hoc reporting exercise; it is a risk management tool produced in parallel with design decisions.

The exam tests whether you know BOTH purposes. An answer that says "documentation supports regulatory compliance" is partially right but incomplete. An answer that says "documentation enables ongoing risk management and compliance accountability" captures both III.A.5 purposes. Also distinguish: III.A.5 (design/build docs) vs. III.B.5 (training/testing docs) vs. III.C.4 (incident documentation).
What to document under III.A.5
Design decisions — architecture choices, model type rationale, trade-offs considered
Risk assessments — harms matrix outputs, risk mitigation options evaluated and selected
Policy application — evidence that ethical considerations, data governance, and human oversight requirements were applied
Stakeholder engagement — records of consultations, feedback received, and how it shaped design
Impact assessment outputs — findings and remediation commitments from III.A.2

Scenario AI-Assisted Hiring Tool — Risk Identification and Documentation ▸
Situation
RetailCo is designing an AI system to screen CVs and rank job applicants for warehouse positions. The HR director has approved the business case and the data science team has begun architecture scoping. Legal and HR business partners have been identified as stakeholders. The governance team must now govern the design phase before any model is built.
Role
You are the AI governance lead. Your mandate is to ensure III.A.4 and III.A.5 are applied before the team finalises model selection.
Analysis
Step 1 — Stakeholder mapping (III.A.4): Extend the stakeholder list beyond HR and Legal to include applicants (data subjects and potential harm recipients), line managers (end-users), regulators (employment and data protection authorities), and advocacy groups for protected categories. Each stakeholder group has a different risk profile.

Step 2 — Harms matrix (III.A.4): Identify potential harms: (a) discriminatory ranking by gender or ethnicity — probability HIGH given training data likely reflects historical hiring patterns; severity HIGH given legal exposure and harm to individuals; (b) false negatives rejecting qualified candidates — probability MEDIUM; severity MEDIUM; (c) CV parsing errors for non-standard formats — probability MEDIUM; severity LOW. The matrix immediately flags harm (a) as the highest priority mitigation target.

Step 3 — Risk mitigation hierarchy (III.A.4): For harm (a), evaluate options top-down: elimination (remove the ranking feature entirely) — possible but would negate the tool's purpose; redesign (use an architecture that explicitly excludes protected-attribute proxies) — feasible; engineering controls (bias detection thresholds, demographic parity checks) — add as a second layer. Do not default to monitoring alone.

Step 4 — Documentation (III.A.5): Record all decisions: stakeholder list rationale, harms matrix with probability/severity ratings, mitigation options considered and selected, and which policies (non-discrimination, data governance) were applied. This documentation serves both compliance (demonstrating policy adherence) and risk management (enabling future audit and remediation). III.A.4 — Identify and manage internal and external risks; III.A.5 — Document the designing and building process
Outcome
The governance team's harms matrix identifies discrimination risk as requiring redesign-level mitigation — not just a monitoring dashboard. The documentation package created during design (not after launch) provides an audit trail that satisfies both regulatory requirements and the organisation's internal risk management framework.
Recall Flashcards — Click to reveal ▸
QIII.A.4 lists six named tools. Name them.
Probability/severity harms matrix · risk mitigation hierarchy · stakeholder mapping · use-case evaluation · benchmarking · pre-deployment pilots and testing.
QWhat are the two explicit purposes of documentation under III.A.5?
To establish compliance AND to manage risks. Both must appear in a complete answer — neither alone is sufficient.
QHow does the risk mitigation hierarchy rank elimination vs. monitoring?
Elimination is the highest tier (most effective). Monitoring and disclosure is the lowest tier. Always prefer the highest feasible tier.
QWhat distinguishes benchmarking (III.A.4) from red teaming (III.C.3)?
Benchmarking happens during design and build — before deployment. Red teaming under III.C.3 is a periodic post-deployment assessment. The key distinction is timing: design phase vs. operational phase.
QWhat distinguishes stakeholder mapping (III.A.4) from stakeholder engagement (III.A.3)?
Mapping identifies who the stakeholders are and their risk exposure. Engagement (III.A.3) is the active consultation and feedback process with those stakeholders during design. Map first, engage second.
QAn AI team finds a potential harm. Should they document it during design or after launch?
During design (III.A.5). Documentation is produced in parallel with design decisions — it is a risk management tool, not a post-deployment report. Waiting until after launch violates III.A.5.
QWhat makes use-case evaluation different from defining the business context (III.A.1)?
III.A.1 defines the approved, intended use case. III.A.4 use-case evaluation stress-tests that scope against edge cases, off-label uses, and misuse scenarios to identify additional risks — it is a risk tool applied to the already-defined use case.
